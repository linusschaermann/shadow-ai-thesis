\section{Theoretical Foundations - 6-8 pages} \label{theoretical-foundations}

In order to establish a comprehensive understanding of \ac{SIT} and its further, \ac{SAI}, this section provides an overview of the theoretical foundations and definitions relevant to these phenomena.

\subsection{Shadow IT} \label{shadow-it}

\subsubsection{Concept and Definition}

\ac{SIT} is a multifaceted phenomenon encompassing the use, development, or deployment of \ac{IT} artifacts (e.g., software, hardware, or services) outside the formal control, approval, or awareness of the central \ac{IT} function. For instance, \citet[1]{haag2017shadow} describe it as "hardware, software, or services built, introduced, and/or used for the job without explicit approval or even knowledge of the organization," whereas \citet[1]{kopper2016towards} emphasize autonomous deployment within business departments without IT involvement. Other authors similarly highlight the lack of alignment with IT governance (\citealp[4]{kaess2021towards}; \citealp[4]{rakovic2020shadow}).

Beyond these operational perspectives, \ac{SIT} can be conceptualized as a structural complement to formal IT, consisting of independently developed and used systems, processes, and practices that are not known, supported, or accepted by the official IT department (\citealp[147]{rakovic2020shadow}). More concise definitions further highlight either the user-driven development outside central IT or the absence of approval and monitoring as defining features (\citealp[4]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[2]{magunduni2018revisiting}).

Collectively, these definitions establish \ac{SIT} as a theoretically grounded construct characterized by autonomy, decentralization, and non-sanctioned IT activity, emerging outside formal IT governance.

\subsubsection{Scope}

\ac{SIT} encompasses a wide range of tools and solutions, including spreadsheets, databases, cloud services, mobile devices, and other ad-hoc \ac{IT} solutions (\citealp[2]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[1]{kopper2016towards}). These systems are typically small-scale, developed by individuals or small teams, and tailored to immediate operational needs \citep[2]{rakovic2020shadow}. Their emergence reflects both the accessibility of modern \ac{IT} tools and the flexibility required by employees in dynamic work environments.

\subsubsection{Conceptual Distinctions}

While \ac{SIT} shares similarities with related practices such as workarounds, and \ac{BYOD} programs, it is distinct in being unauthorized and unsanctioned. Workarounds represent behavioral adaptations to organizational constraints, whereas \ac{SIT} manifests these adaptations through technological artifacts. IT consumerization, on the other hand, refers to the broader trend of user-driven technology adoption, providing a context in which \ac{SIT} can emerge (\citealp[2]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[2]{haag2017shadow}).

\subsubsection{Core Characteristics}

\ac{SIT} is not necessarily malicious or risky in intention. Users often deploy it to enhance productivity, overcome system limitations, or facilitate collaboration. Its defining features are low visibility, lack of \ac{IT} support, and a tension between organizational control and individual initiative.

\section{Methodology - 2-3 pages} \label{methodology}

The review follows a structured approach based on the guidelines provided by \citet[887-902]{okoli2015guide} for conducting a \ac{SLR} in the field of information systems research. The methodology is organized into four phases of two steps each: Planning, Selection, Extraction, and Execution \citep[885]{okoli2015guide}.

While the original framework is designed for collaborative research settings involving multiple reviewers, this review was conducted by a single researcher. Therefore, minor adaptions were made to certain steps (e.g., drafting the research protocol, team training) to ensure methodological rigor while maintaining the integrity of the review process. The following sections describe these phases and their implementation in the context of this review.

\subsection{Purpose} \label{purpose}

The first step in conducting a \ac{SLR} is to define the purpose of the review, which is to identify and analyze existing literature on the phenomenon of \ac{SIT} and further the development of \ac{SAI} in organizational contexts. The \ac{SLR} aims to synthesize insights on the motivations behind \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}, their organizational consequences, and the governance approaches employed to manage these phenomena.

\subsection{Draft Protocol} \label{draft-protocol}

The second step includes drafting a research question, a research protocol, and training the team. Since this thesis is conducted by a single person, the protocol and team training steps are not required and will therefore not be carried out further. The research questions guiding this review are as follows:

\textbf{Main Research Question (RQ):}
How does \ac{SAI} differ from traditional \ac{SIT} in organizations with regard to motivational factors, consequences, and countermeasures?

To better address the research question, it will be broken down into three sub-questions and answered throughout the paper: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Which motivational factors known from \ac{SIT} are transferable to \ac{SAI}, and which additional motivation factors are specific to \ac{SAI}?
  \item Which organizational consequences of \ac{SAI} differ from those of \ac{SIT}?
  \item Which countermeasures are discussed for \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}, and how do they differ in design and expected effectiveness?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Practical Screening} \label{practical-screening}

To make sure only relevant and high-quality literature is included in the review, practical screening criteria are defined to filter the initial search results. 

\begin{itemize}
  \item Content: Shadow IT in Organizations, Shadow AI in Organizations, Motivations for Shadow IT/AI, Organizational Consequences of Shadow IT/AI, Governance of Shadow IT/AI
  \item Language: English
  \item Time Frame: 2005 - present
  \item Publication Type: Journal Articles, Conference Proceedings, Books, Book Chapters/Sections
  \item Setting: Organizational/professional
  \item Participants: Professionals, managers, analysts, data scientists, IT staff, end-users
  \item Design: Empirical (quantitative, qualitative, mixed methods), Theoretical/Conceptual, Review
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Extraction} \label{data-extraction}

The remaining literature was subjected to a systematic data extraction process, where the literature was coded and sorted based on their key scope in accordance to the research questions. Based on this preliminary coding, relevant information was extracted and collected in a structured format to group similar concepts and themes together. This process was conducted iteratively, with multiple rounds of coding and extraction to ensure that all relevant information was captured and organized effectively for further analysis and synthesis in the subsequent phases of the review.

\subsection{Quality Appraisal} \label{quality-appraisal}

While the practical screening criteria help filter out irrelevant studies before even reading the full text, the review process also involves a quality appraisal to ensure the included studies meet certain standards. Therefore exclusion criteria need to be defined and applied to the extracted studies \citep[884]{okoli2015guide}. 

This \ac{SLR} excluded studies with unsufficient theoretical contribution, unclear methodology or findings, or low prerequisites to adequately answer the research questions.
