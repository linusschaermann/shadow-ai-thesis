\lhead{Zusammenfassung}
\section*{Zusammenfassung}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Zusammenfassung}

Der zunehmende Einsatz von künstlicher Intelligenz und den damit verbundenen Werkzeugen am Arbeitsplatz hat ein Phänomen hervorgebracht, das als Schatten-KI bezeichnet wird. Schatten-KI bezeichnet die unauthorisierte Nutzung KI-basierter Anwendungen durch Mitarbeitende ohne das Wissen oder die Genehmigung durch ihre Organisation oder die zuständige Abteilung. Während Schatten-IT, welches die übergeordnete Praxis der unsanktionierten Nutzung verschiedenster IT-Lösungen und IT-Diensten darstellt, in den vergangenen Jahren intensiv erforscht wurde, ist der Zusammenhang zwischen Schatten-IT und Schatten-KI bislang noch unzureichend untersucht worden. Die vorliegende Arbeit addressiert diese Lücke mit der Durchführung einer umfassenden, systematischen Literaturuntersuchung zu den Motivationsfaktoren, Konsequenzen für Organisationen und Gegenmaßnahmen von Schatten-IT und Schatten-KI. Die Ergebnisse dieser Untersuchung dienen als Basis für die Entwicklung eines konzeptuellen Frameworks, das die Erkenntnisse der Schatten-IT Forschung auf den Bereich der Schatten-KI überträgt, gefolgt von einer strukturierten Gegenüberstellung beider Phänomene. Die Ergebnisse der Arbeit zeigen, dass Schatten-KI am treffensten als Weiterentwicklung von Schatten-IT zu verstehen ist, da sich die grundlegenden Treiber für dieses Verhalten (unerfüllter Bedarf, bürokratische Hürden, Leistungsdruck, fehlende Richtlinien/-kenntnisse und IT-Konsumerisierung) von Schatten-IT auf Schatten-KI übertragen lassen. Gleichzeitig lassen sich einige KI-spezifische Motivationsdimensionen identifizieren. Mitarbeiter kompensieren Lücken in ihren Fähigkeiten, wollen dem wettbewerbsbedingten Druck standhalten und finden sich in Führungsstrukturen wieder, in denen formale KI-Richtlinien keine Wirkung entfalten. Beide Phänomene weisen eine Dualität in ihren Konsequenzen aus Risiken und Nutzen auf. Jedoch birgt Schatten-KI einige neuartige und weitreichendere Risiken, die von konventionellen IT-Sicherheitsmechanismen nicht aufgefangen werden können. Hinsichtlich der Gegenmaßnahmen bietet sich ein ähnliches Bild, da Governance-Ansätze für Schatten-IT eine übertragbare Grundlage bilden, Schatten-KI jedoch wesentliche Erweiterungen benötigt, wie KI-spezifische Schutzmaßnahmen oder verhaltensorientierte Governance-Rahmen. Die Arbeit schlussfolgert, dass Organisationen, die das Schattenverhalten ihrer Mitarbeiter als Signal und nicht strikt als Nicht-Einhaltung der Richtlinien behandeln, im Zusammenspiel mit etablierten Schatten-IT Gegenmaßnahmen besser auf die Herausforderungen durch Schatten-KI positioniert sind als solche ohne entsprechende Erfahrung.

\lhead{Abstract}
\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

The increasing use of artificial intelligence and related tools in the workplace has given rise to a phenomenon known as shadow AI. Shadow AI refers to the unauthorized use of AI-based applications by employees without the knowledge or approval of their organization or the relevant department. While shadow IT, which represents the overarching practice of the unsanctioned use of various IT solutions and services, has been extensively researched in recent years, the relationship between shadow IT and shadow AI has not yet been sufficiently explored. This study addresses this gap by conducting a comprehensive, systematic literature review on the motivational factors, consequences for organizations, and countermeasures related to shadow IT and shadow AI. The results of this review serve as the basis for developing a conceptual framework that applies the findings of shadow IT research to the field of shadow AI, followed by a structured comparison of both phenomena. The results of this study show that shadow AI is best understood as an evolution of shadow IT, as the fundamental drivers of this behavior (unmet needs, bureaucratic hurdles, performance pressure, lack of guidelines/knowledge, and IT consumerization) can be transferred from shadow IT to shadow AI. At the same time, several AI-specific motivational factors can be identified. Employees seek to compensate for gaps in their skills, want to withstand competitive pressure, and find themselves in organizational structures where formal AI policies have no effect. Both phenomena exhibit a duality in their consequences, involving both risks and benefits. However, shadow AI poses some novel and far-reaching risks that cannot be mitigated by conventional IT security mechanisms. A similar picture emerges regarding countermeasures, as governance approaches for shadow IT provide a transferable foundation, but shadow AI requires significant extensions, such as AI-specific protective measures or behavior-oriented governance frameworks. The study concludes that organizations that treat their employees' shadow behavior as a signal rather than strictly as non-compliance with policies, in conjunction with established shadow IT countermeasures, are better positioned to address the challenges posed by shadow AI than those without such experience.

\section{Introduction} \label{introduction}

The way employees interact with technology in the workplace changed fundamentally in the recent years. Instead of relying solely on the hardware, software, and services provided by their organization, employees are increasingly using their own technical solutions to get their work done, without the organization's knowledge or approval. This phenomenon, known as \ac{SIT}, is neither insignificant nor unintentional. \cite{gartner2023predictions} reports that 41 percent of employees are already acquiring, modifying or creating technology that \ac{IT} organizations are unaware of; a figure that represents only a share of the projected 75 percent by 2027. The organizational implications of this development are significant. Official \ac{IT} management structures are not prepared to detect or mitigate the risks of unauthorized \ac{IT} use, which increases the attack surface, introduces gaps in data governance, and poses operational risks. At the same time, this behavior also opens up opportunities for increased productivity, accelerated innovation, and empowers employees to meet operational requirements that sanctioned systems fail to support. This duality of organizational risks and opportunities is one of the major challenges modern IT management faces.

With the recent emergence of \ac{AI} technologies, such as generative \ac{AI} and the integration of \ac{AI} into consumer and enterprise applications, a new and more complex variant of \ac{SIT} has emerged: \ac{SAI}. Where \ac{SIT} primarily involves the unauthorized use of hardware, software, and services, \ac{SAI} extends this behavior by drafting communication, analyzing data, supporting decisions, and generating content based on internal data without organizational awareness or governance. The scale of this shift is already empirically visible: 38 percent of employees share sensitive information with \ac{AI} tools without the organization's permission \citep{coker2024third}.

Despite the urgency of the issue in practice, the academic literature has yet to establish a connection between these two phenomena. While \ac{SIT} is already a well-established and recognized phenomenon with a broad body of literature of all kinds (e.g. literature reviews, taxonomies, governance frameworks) across a wide range of research fields, \ac{SAI} is a concept that has not been thoroughly defined and delimited. Especially the relationship between the accumulated knowledge about \ac{SIT} and the construct of \ac{SAI} has not been systematically examined. It therefore remains unclear which findings from the \ac{SIT} literature transfer directly to the \ac{SAI} context, which require adaptation, and which aspects of \ac{SAI} represent novel phenomena to demand entirely new frameworks. This gap is not only academically significant but also practically urgent: organizations confronted with \ac{SAI} for the first time need to know whether they can draw on their \ac{SIT} experience or whether they are facing a fundamentally new challenge.

This thesis addresses that gap. The main research question guiding this thesis is: \textit{How does Shadow AI relate to and differ from traditional Shadow IT
in organizations with regard to motivational factors, consequences, and
countermeasures?} To structure the analysis further, three sub-questions are derived from the main research question. The first asks which motivational factors known from \ac{SIT} are transferable to \ac{SAI}, and which additional motivational factors are specific to the \ac{AI} context. The second sub-question asks what organizational consequences \ac{SAI} produces, and in what ways they differ from or escalate beyond those documented for \ac{SIT}. The third asks which countermeasures have been identified for \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} respectively, and to what extent \ac{SIT} governance approaches are transferable to the \ac{SAI} domain. These questions are addressed through a \ac{SLR}, separately examining the current state of research on \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} in terms of motivational factors, consequences, and countermeasures, followed by a conceptual analysis that extends the \ac{SIT} framework to \ac{SAI} and structured comparative analysis of both phenomena.

Section~\ref{theoretical-foundations} explains the theoretical foundations behind the concept of \ac{SIT}. In doing so, this thesis compares several existing and widely accepted definitions, identifies commonalities and differences, and ultimately develops a comprehensive working definition that serves as the foundation for the following chapters. Section~\ref{methodology} then presents the methodology used and the criteria applied to identify, evaluate, and utilize high-quality academic literature in order to provide a broad overview of the current state of research and draw conclusions on it. The results of the \ac{SLR} are presented in Section~\ref{slr-results}. This section separately examines the current state of research on motivations, consequences, and countermeasures for both \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}. Given the relative novelty of \ac{SAI}, the \ac{SLR} is followed by Section~\ref{conceptual-development}, which presents a conceptual framework that extends the existing findings to the context of \ac{SAI}. Section~\ref{comparative-analysis} them compares the two phenomena in terms of the areas previously examined (motivations, consequences, countermeasures) to identify similarities and differences. Finally, Section~\ref{discussion} discusses the findings and answers the research questions, followed by Section~\ref{limitations} highlighting the limitations of the review and avenues for future research. Section~\ref{conclusion} then concludes the thesis by summarizing the key findings.

\section{Theoretical Foundations} \label{theoretical-foundations}

\subsection{Defining Shadow IT}

\ac{SIT} is not a monolithic or self-evident term but rather a construct that has been progressively defined and refined across multiple research traditions. Defining the phenomenon just by employees using \ac{IT} without organizational approval or awareness is insufficient to capture the full scope of it. Therefore it is necessary to translate the construct into a working definition consisting of multiple research streams to illuminate the distinct facets and dimensions of existing \ac{SIT} definitions.
Early scholarly attempts primarily approached \ac{SIT} from a behavioral perspective, characterizing it as a form of non-compliant user-driven innovation, emphasizing that employees are not passive recipients of organizational \ac{IT} but actively introducing their own technological solutions, even if they are unauthorized \citep[3]{gyoery2012exploring}. Subsequent definitions expanded and shifted toward the artifact itself rather than the behavior. \ac{SIT} has been described as "hardware, software, or services built, introduced, and/or used for the job without explicit approval or even knowledge of the organization", or more compact as technology "brought by the user without approval" \citep[469, 470]{haag2017shadow}. These formulations establish the core characteristics that appear across the definitional literature: the lack of organizational approval, and the absence of organizational awareness.

The most comprehensive and widely adopted definition, proposed by \citet[3]{zimmermann2014emergence} and operationalized in the taxonomy work of \citet[1, 4-5]{kopper2016towards}, captures both core characteristics at the system level: \ac{SIT} includes process-supporting \ac{IT} systems, \ac{IT} services, and \ac{IT} staff deployed autonomously by users or business units. These entities operate outside the organization's formal \ac{IT} management structures. They are neither technically nor strategically integrated and therefore excluded from the organization's official \ac{IT} landscape. \citet[4]{kaess2021towards} made a critical contribution in their taxonomy by identifying covertness as the defining characteristic that distinguishes \ac{SIT} from all conceptually adjacent phenomena.
 
Drawing on these definitional perspectives, this thesis adopts the following working definition of \ac{SIT}:

\begin{quote}
  Shadow IT refers to hardware, software, and services that are deployed, obtained, or used by individual employees or business units to support job-related tasks, without explicit approval, involvement, or awareness of the organizational IT department.
\end{quote}

This definition captures three distinct dimensions present across the literature. First, the artifact dimension, which includes hardware, software and services, reflects the full range of technological resources that employees may introduce (\citealp[469]{haag2017shadow}; \citealp[4]{kaess2021towards}). Second, the user dimension covering both individual employees and entire business units, acknowledges that \ac{SIT} can emerge from different organizational levels and is not limited to isolated user behavior, as confirmed by the taxonomy dimension of \citet[3]{kopper2016towards}. Third, the governance dimension constitutes the boundary condition that separates \ac{SIT} from all overt forms of \ac{BMIT} (\citealp[3]{gyoery2012exploring}; \citealp[469, 470]{haag2017shadow}). For the purpose of this thesis, the definition is therefore strictly applied to covert systems. Once a \ac{SIT} instance is detected, acknowledged, and integrated into the organizational \ac{IT} landscape, it transitions into the category of \ac{BMIT}.

\subsection{Conceptual Distinctions in Terminology}

Having established a working definition of \ac{SIT}, it is important to differentiate it from the various related and adjacent concepts and terms that are often used for similar phenomena or the same terms used for different concepts \citep[1]{kopper2016towards}. For this purpose it is necessary to clarify the conceptual distinctions between those terms and concepts to avoid terminological confusion and ensure a precise understanding of \ac{SIT} as the central construct of this thesis. The following section organizes the relevant concepts hierarchically, from the broadest theoretical category down to specific manifestations and adjacent forms, to locate \ac{SIT} within the broader landscape of organizational \ac{IT} behavior. 

\textit{Feral Practices} represent the broadest conceptual category within the \ac{SIT} domain. The concept refers to any use of \ac{IT} that deviates from organizational standards and exists beyond the control or awareness of formal \ac{IT} management \citep[2]{thatte2012feral}. They essentially function as an umbrella concept under which several more specific concepts can be located, including workarounds and \ac{SIT} itself \citep[3]{kopper2016towards}. Positioning Feral Practices as the overarching category allows for a useful orientation: it shows that the phenomena discussed in this thesis are not isolated instances but part of a broader pattern of non-compliance with formal \ac{IT} governance.

Within the umbrella of Feral Practices, two distinct behavioral forms can be identified: \textit{\ac{SIT}}, which is already defined in the previous section, and \textit{Workarounds}, which describe the alternative, goal-driven use of official and already existing work systems, provided or accepted by the organization, in order to improve efficiency and achieve work-related or personal goals \citep[1044]{alter2014theory}. Critically, the system itself remains authorized and it is only the mode of use that deviates from its intended design. Workarounds are therefore a rather unradical form of non-compliance, as they do not introduce new systems or services but rather repurpose existing ones.

Upon a closer inspection of \ac{SIT}, the term \textit{Shadow Systems} refers specifically to the applications, databases, spreadsheets, and similar tools, that are used in an unauthorized way, without reference to the \ac{IT} infrastructure behind \citep[5]{kopper2016towards}. This narrower scope of Shadow Systems makes it a useful concept where the focus is specifically on unauthorized applications rather than on the broader range of organizational behavior.

Not all concepts adjacent to \ac{SIT} describe deviant behavior directly. \textit{IT Consumerization} describes the trend of employees using their personal devices or applications for job-related tasks \citep[847]{klesel2019employees}. While this does not propose an immediate risk of deviating from organizational standards, it lowers the threshold for unauthorized \ac{IT} use. Employees become increasingly familiar with consumer-grade technologies, their expectations of workplace technology rise, and their willingness to adopt \ac{IT} solutions independently increases. Because of this characteristic, \ac{IT} Consumerization is best not understood as a subtype of \ac{SIT} but as a contextual driver under which \ac{SIT} is more likely to emerge.

Finally, \textit{\ac{BMIT}} takes a position next to \ac{SIT} rather than beneath it. It is used for business \ac{IT} instances used in alignment with the \ac{IT} organization, but managed by the business unit itself \citep[3]{klotz2020shadow}. The critical distinction from \ac{SIT} is transparency. \ac{BMIT} operates within, rather than outside, the formal boundaries of the organization. Therefore it is not a form of \ac{SIT} but rather its counterpart. This relationship is not static: For \ac{SIT} instances, it is possible that they are detected and accepted, which then turns them into \ac{BMIT} \citep[29]{klotz2019causing}. This transition is particularly relevant for the countermeasures discussed in the later sections.

Although there are subtle differences in the various terms and their definitions, all categories share the characteristic of circumventing the organization's official standards and rules or bearing the risk of deviating from them. Together, they form a conceptual ecosystem within which \ac{SIT} holds a specific position, more radical than Workarounds, more concrete than Feral Practices, distinct from its counterpart \ac{BMIT}, and increasingly enabled by the trend of \ac{IT} Consumerization.

\section{Methodology} \label{methodology}

The review follows a structured approach based on the guidelines provided by \citet[887-902]{okoli2015guide} for conducting a \ac{SLR} in the field of information systems research. This framework was selected due to its strong acceptance in the field and its emphasis on methodological transparency and rigor. While the original framework is designed for collaborative research settings involving multiple reviewers, this review was conducted by a single researcher. Therefore, minor adaptions were made to certain steps (e.g., drafting the research protocol, team training) to maintain the integrity of the review process. 

This review follows a concept-centric approach in line with existing and established information systems literature review methodologies. The goal is to organize existing knowledge around key dimensions rather than relying on individual studies. Also the review is primarily qualitative and interpretive in nature, focusing on the synthesis of conceptual and empirical findings. 

The methodology is organized into eight systematic steps to ensure methodological rigor and coverage on the most important sources of literature on the chosen topic \citep[885]{okoli2015guide}. The following sections describe these phases and their implementation in the context of this review.

\subsection{Purpose} \label{purpose}

The first step in conducting a \ac{SLR} is to define the purpose of the review, which is to identify and analyze existing literature on the phenomenon of \ac{SIT} and further the development of \ac{SAI} in organizational contexts. The \ac{SLR} aims to synthesize insights on the motivational drivers behind \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}, their organizational consequences, and the governance approaches employed to manage these phenomena. Those insights will be used to answer the research questions and to derive implications for both research and practice in the organizational management of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}. Beyond summarizing existing literature, this review also seeks to contribute to a conceptual understanding of \ac{SAI} as an extension and transformation of \ac{SIT}.

\subsection{Draft Protocol} \label{draft-protocol}

The second step includes drafting a research question, a research protocol, and training the team. Given the single-researcher setting of this review, the protocol was defined a prori and consistently followed throughout the review process to ensure methodological rigor. Potential bias was mitigated through the consistent documentation of decisions and iteratively reviewing the extracted data. The research questions guiding this review are as follows:

\textbf{Main Research Question (RQ):}
How does \ac{SAI} relate to and differ from traditional \ac{SIT} in organizations with regard to motivational factors, consequences, and countermeasures?

To better address the research question, it will be broken down into three sub-questions and answered throughout the paper: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Which motivational factors known from \ac{SIT} are transferable to \ac{SAI}, and which additional motivation factors are specific to \ac{SAI}?
  \item Which organizational consequences does \ac{SAI} produce, and in what ways do they differ from or escalate beyond those documented for \ac{SIT}?
  \item Which countermeasures have been identified for \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} respectively, and to what extent are \ac{SIT} governance approaches transferable to \ac{SAI}?
\end{enumerate}

These dimensions were selected as they offer a comprehensive overview of the phenomena as wholes, covering the underlying drivers, the resulting organizational implications, and the potential responses. This structure also allows for a systematic comparison between \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} along these key dimensions, which is essential to be able to answer the main research question.

\subsection{Practical Screening} \label{practical-screening}

To make sure only relevant and high-quality literature is included in the review, practical screening criteria are defined to filter the large number of initial search results before reviewing them in detail.

For this review, only journal articles, conference proceedings, and book chapters/sections published in English between 2005 and 2026 with a focus on \ac{SIT}/\ac{SAI} in organizational or professional settings were included. This time frame was chosen to capture the emergence and evolution of mostly \ac{SIT}, as \ac{SAI} is a more recent phenomenon. The research design of the included studies could be empirical (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods), theoretical, conceptual, or review-based, as long as they provide insights relevant to the research questions. The participants in the included studies could be professionals, managers, employees in various roles (e.g., analysts, data scientists, \ac{IT} staff), or end-users as long as they have a close connection to the organizational context of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}. Additionally, non-scholarly sources such as blog posts or personal opinions were excluded to maintain the academic rigor.

\subsection{Literature Search} \label{literature-search}

The search process was completed on March 15, 2026, and therefore includes only literature published up to that date.

\subsection{Data Extraction} \label{data-extraction}

The remaining literature was subjected to a systematic data extraction process, where the literature was coded and sorted based on their key scope in accordance to the research questions. Based on this preliminary coding, relevant information was extracted and collected in a structured format to group similar concepts and themes together. This process was conducted iteratively, with multiple rounds of coding and extraction to ensure that all relevant information was captured and organized effectively for further analysis and synthesis in the subsequent phases of the review. For each included study, key information such as publication details, research design, and core findings related to the research questions were extracted. The extraction and coding was documented in a structured format (spreadsheets and notes).

\subsection{Quality Appraisal} \label{quality-appraisal}

While the practical screening criteria help filter out irrelevant studies before even reading the full-text, the review process also involves a quality appraisal to ensure the included studies meet certain standards. Therefore exclusion criteria need to be defined and applied to the extracted studies \citep[884]{okoli2015guide}. 

This \ac{SLR} excluded studies with insufficient theoretical contribution, unclear methodology or findings, or low prerequisites to adequately answer the research questions.

\subsection{Study Synthesization} \label{study-synthesization}

Study synthesization involves aggregating, summarizing, and interpreting the findings from the included studies to answer the research questions \citep[899]{okoli2015guide}. The aggregation utilized the preliminary outline of the review, which was based on the research questions, to group similar concepts and themes, such as definitions, motivations, consequences, and countermeasures of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}. The concept-centric perspective was applied to systematically organize findings across studies rather than summarizing them individually.

\subsection{Writing the Review} \label{writing-the-review}

The final phase of the review process presents the results in detail, so future reviewers can understand and reproduce the process and the findings \citep[901]{okoli2015guide}. To further enhance transparency and reproducibility, a detailed explanation of how and at which stages \ac{AI} tools were used in the preparation of this thesis is provided in Appendix~\ref{appendix-a}. Section~\ref{slr-results} presents the results of the \ac{SLR}, organized around the key themes of identified literature, motivational factors, organizational consequences, and governance approaches of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} to summarize existing literature and knowledge about these concepts.

\subsection{Motivational Factors} \label{motivational-factors}

A clear understanding of the motivational factors behind \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} is not only important to explain their emergence, but also for developing effective countermeasures. Governance approaches that focus only on prohibition or technical restriction often fail because they address the symptoms rather than the underlying causes. 

\subsubsection{Shadow IT}

The motivational drivers of \ac{SIT} are multifaceted, spanning individual, organizational, and technological dimensions. As a systematic review establishes, there is always an underlying need for end users to complete their jobs that ultimately prompts unauthorized \ac{IT} adoption \citep[148]{rakovic2020shadow}, and a comprehensive framework situates these drivers within three overarching categories: enablers, motivators, and missing barriers \citep[22]{klotz2019causing}.

The most fundamental driver is the gap between what employees require and what the \ac{IT} department provides. Dissatisfaction with officially sanctioned systems, combined with performance pressure, leads employees to seek unauthorized alternatives \citep[233, 235]{pinto2023encourage}, and \ac{SIT} fills precisely this void between users and official \ac{IT} solutions \citep[1023]{silic2017perspective}. Employees frequently perceive shadow tools as more useful than formal alternatives \citep[3]{mallmann2016behavioral}, particularly when official systems fail to align with actual job routines or adequate applications are simply unavailable \citep[1]{nwankpa2024shadow}. \ac{SIT} also addresses entirely new functional requirements not yet covered by central \ac{IT} \citep[2]{chua2014central}, emerging as a direct result of unmet demand from functional units \citep[11]{chua2014central}. Employees with unfulfilled technological needs actively seek and independently implement solutions \citep[5]{klotz2019transformed}, and the inability to fulfill business needs through legitimate channels is a primary structural driver of \ac{SIT} \citep[6]{gyoery2012exploring}. This is compounded when business units lack the power to influence central \ac{IT} priorities \citep[3]{furstenau2016shadow}, and unacknowledged grievances remain among the most cited causes of \ac{SIT} across contexts \citep[653]{walterbusch2017missing}.

Workaround behavior and \ac{SIT} usage positively impact individual performance by enabling faster task completion \citep[239]{pinto2023encourage}, and represent a source of broader process innovation \citep[114]{white2023workarounds}. Employees credit shadow tools with flexibility and creativity in task accomplishment \citep[1171]{trialih2023challenge} and with enabling remote data access and extended working hours \citep[2]{koch2014consumerization}. Shadow systems offer the customization that rigid legacy environments deny \citep[108]{myers2017impact}, though these short-term productivity gains can simultaneously create cybersecurity vulnerabilities \citep[1]{manga2025cracks}.

\ac{IT} organization slowness is the single most prominent motivator of \ac{SIT} in practitioner studies, yet remains underrepresented in academic literature \citep[16]{kopper2019shadowit}. Excessively long response times and inadequate \ac{IT} support are principal reasons employees turn to unofficial solutions \citep[3]{keskin2021domesticating}, and pursuing official channels demands strict adherence to formal development procedures and approval processes \citep[9]{chua2014central}. From a transaction cost perspective, \ac{SIT} emerges when transacting with the \ac{IT} department becomes more costly than self-provisioning \citep[7]{zimmermann2014managing}, and employees naturally gravitate toward whichever \ac{IT} alternative carries the lowest cost \citep[4]{lueker2016consumerization}.

Autonomy in the workplace directly drives both workaround and \ac{SIT} behavior \citep[239]{pinto2023encourage}, and the ability to implement systems quickly and independently is the most prominent motivator in qualitative studies \citep[5]{klotz2019transformed}. Business units that consider themselves more capable than central \ac{IT} act on this by independently procuring systems and refuse to compromise for organizational standardization \citep[16]{kopper2019shadowit}. \ac{SIT} in this form depends entirely on user functions' ability to self-provision \citep[7]{chua2014central}, and departments adopt it fundamentally to support their own processes \citep[1]{zimmermann2014managing}, motivated by the organizational agility self-managed solutions provide \citep[12]{kopper2018business}.

Employees who find official systems stressful adopt shadow tools as coping mechanisms \citep[115]{white2023workarounds}. When organizations fail to provide adequate resources to meet prescribed goals, structural strain arises and drives employees toward unauthorized solutions \citep[5]{thatte2012feral}, with cognitive strain confirmed as a significant determinant of cloud-based \ac{SIT} and work overload identified as an independently significant structural precursor \citep[74, 76]{ogedengbe2024influence}. Beyond individual pressures, social influence and social presence are significant antecedents of \ac{SIT} \citep[6467]{mallmann2019social}, and employees are strongly influenced by peer behavior and community norms in their adoption decisions \citep[12]{mallmann2018weare}. Supervisors who implicitly expect high efficiency may inadvertently incentivize unauthorized tool use \citep[13]{mallmann2018weare}, and \ac{SIT} spreads from individual adopters to entire workgroups following social contagion dynamics \citep[14]{mallmann2018weare}. Employees also adopt tools that enhance social presence across activities including information exchange, decision-making, and conflict resolution \citep[428]{mallmann2021mediating}, with social drivers operating across individual, managerial, and business-level dimensions simultaneously \citep[4]{godefroid2021it}.

The proliferation of consumer-grade tools has increased employees' technological knowledge and lowered adoption barriers \citep[8]{thatte2012feral}. Cloud services require no expert skills to operate \citep[436]{mallmann2021mediating} and offer universal access to work data from any device and location \citep[644]{walterbusch2017missing}; when organizations fail to accommodate these expectations, employees fulfill them independently \citep[653]{walterbusch2017missing}. Awareness of superior consumer alternatives is itself a motivational force \citep[321]{junglas2019innovation}. A significant share of \ac{SIT} behavior stems not from deliberate defiance but from unawareness of policies: carelessness, poor business-\ac{IT} alignment, and organizational culture are among the most cited drivers of non-compliance, with familiarity with security policies and management influence among the strongest predictors \citep[2, 10]{silvius2012factors}. Intentional non-compliance typically occurs only when employees feel restricted or are explicitly encouraged to circumvent rules by supervisors \citep[12]{silvius2012factors}. Employees frequently persist in \ac{SIT} simply because they do not understand applicable policies \citep[277-278]{silic2014shadow}, and most do not perceive their behavior as harmful or illegal \citep[279]{silic2014shadow}, a pattern rooted in deficient communication of \ac{IT} policy \citep[14-15]{mallmann2018weare}.

Performance expectancy, effort expectancy, and subjective norms are the primary predictors of \ac{SIT} usage intention, while personal norms and formal sanctions exert no significant influence \citep[500]{nguyen2024understanding}. Effort expectancy is the single strongest predictor of \ac{SIT} deviance \citep[510]{nguyen2024understanding}: employees who believe adoption will be technically straightforward are significantly more inclined to act on the intention \citep[506-507]{nguyen2024understanding}. Conversely, low self-efficacy with complex official tools reduces perceived policy value and lowers resistance to unauthorized alternatives \citep[4]{welck2017empowerment}. Organizational and individual inertia perpetuate \ac{SIT} by embedding behavioral habits that resist redirection \citep[114]{silic2019critical}. At the institutional level, limited \ac{IT} knowledge within business units, poor business-\ac{IT} communication, and cost pressures within departments all create conditions conducive to \ac{SIT} \citep[5-7]{furstenau2016shadow}, while workarounds arise from obstacles to task completion or goal misalignment between stakeholders \citep[2]{roeder2014managers}. \ac{SIT} also represents active resistance to mandated systems \citep[3]{magunduni2018revisiting}, implemented to address individual or group needs rather than those of the organization as a whole \citep[5]{magunduni2018revisiting}. Finally, remote work amplifies these motivations: the absence of peer monitoring reduces perceived compliance norms, remote settings provide a convenient external attribution for rule-breaking, and employees who lack guilt over deviant behavior face lower social inhibition against non-compliance \citep[4, 5, 7]{n2023information}.

\subsubsection{Shadow AI}

The motivational drivers of \ac{SAI} bear a structural resemblance to those documented for \ac{SIT}, yet are shaped by the distinctive characteristics of \ac{AI} technology itself. At the most fundamental level, employees are motivated by a pressing demand for productivity gains, using unsanctioned \ac{AI} tools to significantly enhance output, automate routine tasks, and augment decision-making \citep[2, 3]{ross2025shadowai}. Generative \ac{AI} in particular is adopted for concrete time-saving purposes, drafting emails, summarizing documents, and brainstorming ideas, that official tools do not readily support \citep[4]{ross2025shadowai}. This efficiency orientation is consistently confirmed across studies: \ac{AI} is widely perceived as beneficial for automating repetitive tasks and providing analytical insights \citep[7]{silic2025shadow}, with interviewees describing \ac{AI}-powered tools as transformative for workflow efficiency \citep[9]{silic2025shadow}, and employees in metaverse-based business contexts claiming that \ac{SAI} enables them to be more efficient, productive, and innovative \citep[2]{chin2025conflicting}. Productivity gains are also a key driver in sensitive industries: employees in healthcare, finance, and education seek out third-party \ac{AI} tools to enhance performance \citep[2]{balogun2025ethical}, driven by the promise of greater efficiency and agility across those sectors \citep[16]{balogun2025ethical}. The broader productivity proposition is further reinforced by \ac{AI} tools' promise of greater efficiency and innovation and new opportunities for competitive advantage \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}.

A second major motivational cluster mirrors the unmet needs dynamic established in the \ac{SIT} literature. \ac{SAI} emerges as a direct response to perceived inefficiencies in officially sanctioned digital infrastructures \citep[2]{ross2025shadowai}, driven by unmet needs, bureaucratic inertia, and a lack of accessible sanctioned alternatives \citep[6]{ross2025shadowai}. Like \ac{SIT} before it, \ac{SAI} is fueled by bureaucratic bottlenecks, centralized system unresponsiveness, and misalignment between official \ac{IT} infrastructure and actual operational needs \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}. Its informal adoption is frequently an operational necessity arising from limited technical resources \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}, and governance frameworks often lag behind employee practices, creating a governance drift zone where formal policies exist but carry no real-world traction \citep[1]{silic2025shadow}. Many workers feel that the agility gained through \ac{AI} experimentation justifies bypassing slower official approval processes, and employees increasingly bypass traditional \ac{IT} procurement altogether to adopt sophisticated LLMs for daily tasks \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}. \ac{SAI} emerges in practice where employees circumvent formal \ac{IT} protocols for personal productivity gains, a dynamic not fully captured by existing adoption frameworks \citep[3]{silic2025shadow}. Shadow systems recurrently emerge due to policy vacuums, \ac{IT} bottlenecks, or employee frustration \citep[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}, and vague approval mechanisms and low policy awareness further facilitate risk-taking behavior \citep[1140]{raizada2026shadowai}. Employees who lack formal guidance or perceive policies as absent will frequently conceal their \ac{AI} usage \citep[2]{varshini2025facing}.

Behavioral and cultural factors form a third distinct motivational dimension. \ac{SAI} usage is predominantly efficiency-driven rather than malicious, especially under task pressure \citep[1140]{raizada2026shadowai}, and the psychological factors of perceived productivity and psychological safety frequently outweigh compliance concerns for the average employee \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}. Employees who experience task pressure report significantly different perceptions of workload when using unsanctioned \ac{AI} tools \citep[5]{varshini2025facing}, and \ac{AI} has been credited with sparking creativity and enabling new approaches to problem-solving \citep[9]{silic2025shadow}. Employees also turn to unsanctioned \ac{AI} to fill gaps in official resources and innovate beyond existing constraints \citep[1]{varshini2025facing}. Adoption is further fueled by a culture of digital improvisation in which employees feel compelled to find their own solutions \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}, shaped by cultural desires for innovation, resistance to bureaucracy, and the informal networks through which digital tools are shared \citep[3]{ross2025shadowai}. This dynamic creates a culture where innovation effectively bypasses control, making top-down prohibitions largely ineffective and driving usage further underground \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}, with innovation-seeking and psychological safety identified as the key behavioral drivers of \ac{AI} experimentation with unmonitored tools \citep[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}. This dynamic creates a culture where innovation effectively bypasses control, making top-down prohibitions largely ineffective and driving usage further underground \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}, with innovation-seeking and psychological safety identified as the key behavioral drivers of \ac{AI} experimentation with unmonitored tools \citep[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}.

Accessibility and skill gap considerations round out the motivational picture. \ac{SAI} tools are not restricted to data science specialists, marketing, human resources, and engineering employees adopt them equally, and the availability of ready-made models in repositories enables non-experts to rapidly prototype and deploy sophisticated models without technical expertise \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}. Employees also resort to consumer-grade \ac{AI} solutions to compensate for skill gaps within their teams \citep[1-2]{ross2025shadowai} and use \ac{AI} skills to offset specific technical weaknesses \citep[3]{an2024impact}. In the era of the digital economy, rapid technological innovation and globalization make \ac{AI} adoption a competitive necessity for organizational survival \citep[1]{an2024impact}, and the broad range of tasks to which digital agents can be applied, from decision-making and communication to information processing and value creation \citep[11]{alter2024updated}, further explains why employees perceive \ac{AI} adoption as an individual imperative even absent organizational sanctioning. The \ac{BYOAI} context, moreover, links user motivations directly to governance design, as a structured mapping of motivations to governance responses reveals clear patterns in how employee needs drive both adoption and the demand for legitimate alternatives \citep[2708, 2709]{anthuvan2025byoai}.

\subsection{Organizational Consequences} \label{organizational-consequences}

Beyond understanding why \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} emerge, it is equally important to examine their organizational consequences. Once adopted, these phenomena can significantly affect organizations, their processes, structures, and governance arrangements. Therefore, it is essential to understand their broader implications and to assess how organizations can respond effectively once they are detected.

\subsubsection{Shadow IT}

\ac{SIT} consequences are fundamentally dualistic, generating both significant organizational risks and genuine performance benefits. Research documents outcomes ranging from security breaches and governance failures to productivity gains and innovation, and \ac{SIT} instances can function either as replacements for official solutions or as complementary additions, depending on context \citep[1025]{rentrop2012shadow}. \ac{IT} managers must therefore balance these competing consequences when formulating governance responses \citep[13]{mallmann2018weare}.

\ac{SIT} undermines official systems and can damage organizational data and processes \citep[1025]{silic2017perspective}, while unauthorized workarounds compromise information security more broadly \citep[114]{white2023workarounds}. Three structural mechanisms generate exposure: shadow systems fall outside \ac{IT} security oversight, they expand the organizational attack surface, and sensitive data stored or transmitted through unsecured tools creates material breach risk \citep[1]{nwankpa2024shadow}. Cloud shadow tools fall outside \ac{IT} maintenance cycles, leaving security vulnerabilities from malware and spyware undetected \citep[653]{walterbusch2017missing}, and employees who bypass formal systems are more susceptible to phishing attacks \citep[1]{manga2025cracks}. \ac{SIT} is consequently one of the most significant concerns in organizational information security, with employees' unawareness of potential harm compounding the problem \citep[500]{nguyen2024understanding}, and practitioners identify data security as its most significant risk \citep[10]{kopper2019shadowit}. Unauthorized cloud use creates monetary and reputational damage risk \citep[644-645]{walterbusch2017missing} and potential GDPR violations, data loss, and exposure to cyberattacks when sensitive data is processed on third-party servers \citep[400]{haag2024dealing}. Unapproved software can introduce malware, and information leakage represents a pervasive privacy risk \citep[280]{silic2014shadow}, with data integrity and information leakage recognized as the highest \ac{IT} risks associated with \ac{SIT} \citep[278]{silic2014shadow}. Unauthorized device use also elevates intellectual property loss risk \citep[3]{pirani2014consumerization}, and non-compliance carries direct legal consequences including fines and personal liability for senior management \citep[1]{silvius2012factors}. User non-compliance with \ac{IT} policy is widely considered the single largest information security threat facing organizations \citep[6]{gyoery2012exploring}.

\ac{SIT} results in a loss of managerial control, reduced productivity, and deviations from intended business processes \citep[1]{roeder2014managers}, threatening the performance improvements organizations expect from official IS investments and creating hazards and errors in critical workflows (\citealp[1, 3]{roeder2014managers}; \citealp[4]{shaikh2021shadow}). The cumulative effect is an elevated organizational risk profile and expanding technical debt \citep[115]{white2023workarounds}. At the organizational level, \ac{SIT} generates inefficiencies through lost synergies, fragmented \ac{IT} landscapes, data inconsistencies, and integration problems \citep[3, 8]{kopper2018business}. A comprehensive catalogue of inadequately managed \ac{SIT} consequences includes time loss, rework, unconsolidated investments, data losses, infrastructure incompatibilities, audit findings, standardization deficits, erosion of trust in \ac{IT} systems, and service interruptions \citep[283]{ozkan2021controlling}. Indirect costs include potentially unrecoverable organizational data loss \citep[75]{ogedengbe2024influence}.

As shadow systems grow, \ac{IT} decentralization frequently follows as a direct organizational consequence, shifting control from central \ac{IT} to business units \citep[6]{furstenau2016shadow}. \ac{IT} departments cannot feasibly monitor every device and application employees wish to adopt \citep[3]{koch2014consumerization}, and \ac{BYOD} and workaround practices force \ac{IT} departments to delegate management responsibility to users while still bearing responsibility for environmental security \citep[2]{dulipovici2016byod}. Shadow systems often grow beyond the capacity of the originating department to maintain, yet remain undetected for extended periods, and are frequently only discovered when multiple departments have independently developed similar solutions \citep[9-10]{koch2014consumerization}. Governance responsibility evolves dynamically over the lifecycle of a shadow instance \citep[10]{klotz2020shadow}, and the reverse transition from \ac{BMIT} back to \ac{SIT} is rare, as it would require stakeholders to progressively lose awareness of the system \citep[12]{klotz2020shadow}. Paradoxically, strict prohibition may push \ac{SIT} deeper into organizational secrecy rather than reducing it \citep[472]{haag2017shadow}.

\ac{SIT} also generates genuine positive consequences. It can function as an efficient substitute for formal systems and mediate the relationship between workarounds and improved individual performance (\citealp[242]{pinto2023encourage}; \citealp[1025]{silic2017perspective}), providing positive value precisely where official \ac{IT} constraints impede job performance \citep[739]{haag2019acceptance}. \ac{SIT} can boost organizational efficiency and effectiveness \citep[1171]{trialih2023challenge}, and empirical cases document measurable productivity improvements and explicit recognition of innovative value by \ac{IT} managers \citep[8]{kopper2018business}. \ac{BMIT} is associated with productivity gains, innovation, and improvements in agility and cross-unit collaboration \citep[4]{godefroid2021it}, and employee feedback arising from unmet needs can serve as a productive signal for successive improvement of official systems \citep[653]{walterbusch2017missing}. Shadow systems also enable faster, more effective communication and collaboration with employees, clients, and partners \citep[26]{mallmann2018influence}, and empirical evidence confirms that social presence mediates the relationship between \ac{SIT} usage and individual performance, with \ac{SIT} having a significant positive relationship with perceived sensitivity and comprehension in collaboration \citep[427]{mallmann2021mediating}. The social dimension also explains how \ac{SIT} facilitates broader knowledge dissemination across organizational boundaries \citep[6468]{mallmann2019social}, and shadow solutions additionally support knowledge sharing by enabling faster information exchange with internal and external partners \citep[2]{klotz2019transformed}. Radical innovations may need to deliberately violate organizational standards in order to succeed \citep[1]{roeder2014managers}, and shadow systems that address operational gaps can evolve into parallel \ac{IT} infrastructures developed faster and more practically than official processes allow \citep[3]{keskin2021domesticating}. \ac{BYOD}-enabled workarounds can likewise produce demonstrable business process improvements first at the individual and then at the organizational level \citep[2]{dulipovici2016byod}, and flexible \ac{IT} strategies such as \ac{BYOD} can enhance individual \ac{IT} innovation capacity irrespective of uptake \citep[373]{koeffer2015innovation}.

Reports produced by shadow systems are consistently perceived as less credible than those from official systems, with direct implications for managerial decision quality \citep[106]{myers2017impact}. At the individual level, employees who use \ac{SIT} receive significantly lower bonus assessments than those using company-endorsed tools, even when outcomes are equivalent, and \ac{SIT} use is likely to be viewed negatively in subjective performance evaluations regardless of work quality \citep[30]{allen2019shadow}. Complementarily, policies accommodating \ac{IT} consumerization are associated with higher policy understanding but lower satisfaction, and compliance behavior is shaped by both procedural justice and rational cost-benefit calculations, reflecting the fundamental tension between organizational control and individual autonomy that defines the \ac{SIT} consequence landscape \citep[1]{lueker2016consumerization}.

\subsubsection{Shadow AI}

The consequences of \ac{SAI} span cybersecurity vulnerabilities, regulatory and ethical failures, governance erosion, and organizational knowledge risks, while also encompassing more nuanced impacts on workplace trust and culture. The proliferation of \ac{SAI} systems poses significant cybersecurity risks, including data breaches, compliance violations, and operational disruptions \citep[1]{whitford2025aidriven}, with key risk domains including data leakage, model hallucination, and compliance breaches identified empirically \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}. At the technical level, shadow backdoor models can be engineered to facilitate adversarial attacks, while shadow data leak models can channel sensitive business information to unauthorized recipients \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}. \ac{SAI} introduces systemic vulnerabilities that challenge traditional cybersecurity models \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai} and expands the attack surface for cybercriminals, making it a critical concern for security professionals \citep[5]{balogun2025ethical}. Unauthorized \ac{AI} usage also raises data sovereignty concerns, as sensitive information is likely to be moved to and stored in locations that do not meet regulatory standards \citep[2]{puthal2025shadowai}.

The regulatory and ethical consequences of \ac{SAI} are particularly acute in high-stakes sectors. In finance, unregulated \ac{AI}-driven credit scoring systems have produced discriminatory lending practices and attracted regulatory scrutiny, and the unchecked expansion of \ac{SAI} risks entrenching systemic biases that further marginalize vulnerable populations \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}. In healthcare, unregulated \ac{AI} diagnostic tools risk patient confidentiality breaches, misdiagnoses, and legal liability for institutions, while in education, unauthorized \ac{AI} contributes to academic dishonesty, flawed grading, and violations of student privacy \citep[2]{balogun2025ethical}. \ac{SAI} also exposes responsibility gaps in high-risk fields: the black-box nature of many \ac{AI} systems makes it difficult to determine how decisions are made, undermining accountability (\citealp[1]{silic2025shadow}; \citealp[4]{balogun2025ethical}). At the organizational level, \ac{SAI} leads to a governance drift zone in which formal policies lack real-world enforcement traction \citep[1]{silic2025shadow}, and the deployment of \ac{BYOAI} creates structural tensions between innovation, trust, and regulatory oversight \citep[2693]{anthuvan2025byoai}.

Beyond technical and regulatory exposure, \ac{SAI} carries significant organizational and social consequences. Employees' use of \ac{SAI} not only generates operational benefits but also poses risks of organizational knowledge leakage \citep[1]{chin2025conflicting}, as sensitive organizational data processed through unsanctioned tools risks exposure outside the organizational boundary. \ac{SAI} also affects workplace culture and trust, with repercussions for team dynamics and the perceived trustworthiness of colleagues \citep[1]{varshini2025facing}. These social and cultural consequences extend the impact of \ac{SAI} beyond the \ac{IT} security domain into the relational fabric of organizational life, underscoring that governance responses must address not only technical risks but also the human and cultural dimensions of unauthorized \ac{AI} adoption.

\subsection{Governance Approaches and Countermeasures} \label{governance-approaches-and-countermeasures}

After exploring why \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} emerge and how they affect organizations, this section explores how the risks of the unauthorized use of \ac{IT} and \ac{AI} tools can be mitigated without losing their potential benefits. Effective governance requires approaches that address underlying drivers while balancing control with innovation.

\subsubsection{Shadow IT}

Governing \ac{SIT} requires \ac{IT} managers to first understand its mechanisms, causes, and consequences before any effective response can be formulated (\citealp[469]{haag2017shadow}; \citealp[6467]{mallmann2019social}). The literature consistently establishes that no single countermeasure is sufficient and that effective governance requires a portfolio of complementary approaches spanning deterrence, governance frameworks, technical controls, formalization strategies, and cultural change.

A foundational finding is that purely restrictive approaches are largely ineffective. Research shows no statistically significant difference between policies that prohibit consumer device use and a complete absence of policy, leading to the recommendation that organizations implement policies that explicitly permit rather than prohibit private device use \citep[856]{klesel2019employees}. Organizations cannot rely solely on regulations to neutralize \ac{SIT}, and sanctions risk being counterproductive when employees adopt \ac{SIT} in good faith to complete their work \citep[1171]{trialih2023challenge}: a pure punishment focus has been shown to be ineffective in hindering violation behavior \citep[511]{nguyen2024understanding}. Prohibition through information security policies is likely to produce abuse rather than compliance \citep[2]{welck2017empowerment}. The technical extreme, eliminating discretionary budgets, locking down devices, and blocking internet access, is neither feasible nor acceptable in most modern organizations \citep[11]{chua2014central}. Since \ac{SIT} represents a form of decentralized computing outside \ac{IT} service management \citep[428]{mallmann2021mediating}, \ac{IT} leaders must instead accept that \ac{SIT} cannot be entirely controlled or eliminated as new digital technologies emerge \citep[408]{haag2024dealing}.

Despite these limitations, sanctions form one legitimate component of a broader governance strategy. Formal sanctions, explicit penalties such as salary reductions or negative performance reviews, provide direct deterrence, complemented by informal sanctions in the form of social peer disapproval for norm violations and the deterrent effect of shame when wrongdoings become known to others \citep[1024, 1026, 1027]{silic2017perspective}. In documented cases, users were notified of non-compliant actions via email, unauthorized software was removed by \ac{IT}, and users were reported to line managers \citep[1029]{silic2017perspective}. Sanctions are most effective when paired with rewards for compliance, with the advantages of conformity actively communicated and the costs of non-compliance, including monetary penalties or dismissal, made explicit (\citealp[11]{roeder2014managers}; \citealp[739]{haag2019acceptance}). Social pressure generated when unauthorized behavior is perceived as undesirable significantly reduces the likelihood of \ac{SIT} being triggered, and an ethical organizational climate strengthens perceptions of both formal and informal controls while facilitating security policy adherence \citep[506, 500, 510]{nguyen2024understanding}.

Most organizations deploy formal risk management tools, \ac{IT} service management, \ac{IT} governance, and security management, to direct and control business unit \ac{IT} activity, with documented responses including formal compliance policies, the appointment of process owners, \ac{IT} budget consolidation, and deliberate reduction of business unit \ac{IT} spending \citep[1, 7]{furstenau2016shadow}. At the portfolio level, governance distinguishes between general governance applying to all \ac{SIT} and instance governance for identified \ac{BMIT} systems, with instance governance allocatable to the \ac{IT} organization, shared between \ac{IT} and the business unit, or retained within the business unit \citep[17, 30]{klotz2019causing}. Once a shadow instance is identified, organizations must decide whether to decommission or continue it, and if continued, assign governance to \ac{IT}, the business unit, or a shared model \citep[3]{kopper2019shadowit}. Three strategic governance postures have been identified: the \ac{IT}-control approach, which centralizes all \ac{IT} budgets and compliance; the user-driven approach, which redistributes \ac{IT} innovation responsibility to business users; and the user-oriented approach, which synthesizes both while maintaining transparency and permitting a healthy extent of \ac{SIT} as an innovation enabler \citep[8-9]{gyoery2012exploring}. Managers must ultimately choose between near-total repeal of restrictive policies or rigorous compliance enforcement \citep[739]{haag2019acceptance}. A co-governance model, allocating task responsibilities between \ac{IT} and the business unit based on risk and transaction cost dimensions, has been proposed as a resolution, as controlled \ac{SIT} simultaneously reduces risk and increases efficiency \citep[9]{zimmermann2016governing}. Organizations should also divide IS into subtasks and allocate responsibilities accordingly, transferring hardware and database components to \ac{IT} while preserving business creativity \citep[5, 8]{kopper2018business}.

Practical technical controls range from simple web and network traffic monitoring to strict domain policies preventing software installation without \ac{IT} authorization \citep[278]{silic2014shadow}. At the technical frontier, machine learning applied to time-aggregated network traffic features has demonstrated promising NAT detection capability, with time window duration shown to significantly affect detection accuracy \citep[6]{nassar2021identifying}. The establishment of a company-wide cloud security strategy, combined with an anonymous employee survey to map current exposure, is recommended as a foundational governance step \citep[644]{walterbusch2017missing}. Monitoring and identification are strategically important because rendering shadow instances overt transitions them from uncontrolled systems into governed \ac{BMIT} \citep[29-30]{klotz2019causing}. Early \ac{IT} departments also employed direct technical controls such as hardware and software standardization and active removal of unauthorized applications from networks, though such measures are insufficient as standalone strategies \citep[9]{koch2014consumerization}.

Among the most constructive countermeasures is the active formalization of identified shadow systems. Organizations must officially recognize, accept, and transfer shadow instances to a controlled area, channeling the energy driving \ac{SIT} into formally supported Business \ac{IT} that improves processes \citep[4]{keskin2021domesticating}. When effective organizational rules are combined with education, training, awareness, and monitoring, employees transition from unauthorized to authorized use; absent these complements, unauthorized behavior persists, and the trigger for transition differs depending on the employee's current behavioral state \citep[6-7, 5]{pirani2014consumerization}. Assessment of a shadow system's innovative potential and its relationship to existing enterprise infrastructure should inform formalization decisions, with risk-critical instances prioritized for integration with official systems \citep[150, 151]{rakovic2020shadow}. Although \ac{BYOD} policies and ratification of unauthorized technologies represent attempts to govern \ac{SIT} through formalization, they are acknowledged to be more complex in practice than in principle \citep[427]{mallmann2021mediating}.

Organizations must continuously improve employee awareness of \ac{IT} security risks and applicable policies \citep[12]{silvius2012factors}, as non-compliance risk is materially mitigated by training, clearly communicated policies, and regular security audits \citep[6]{gyoery2012exploring}. Stricter controls combined with better education and simpler policies would make \ac{SIT} risk mitigation significantly more successful \citep[279]{silic2014shadow}, and cloud-specific risks must be addressed through dedicated training programs \citep[654]{walterbusch2017missing}. Foundational cybersecurity compliance practices include hiring employees with a security-positive orientation, demonstrating leadership commitment, building practical skills through training, supporting compliance follow-through, and communicating the benefits of compliance proactively \citep[195]{cram2020maximizing}. Policy compliance is more reliably achieved when the development process and content of policies are made transparent through active communication \citep[3]{lueker2016consumerization}, and \ac{IT} managers must create initiatives that actively engage users in security policies \citep[26]{mallmann2018influence}.

The \ac{IT} department shares responsibility for \ac{SIT} by failing to provide appropriate systems for employees \citep[26]{mallmann2018influence}, and understanding the antecedents of \ac{SIT} offers \ac{IT} managers an opportunity to proactively provide suitable tools \citep[14]{mallmann2018weare}. Acting fast and anticipating user needs is one of the most effective practitioner strategies for reducing \ac{SIT} adoption \citep[14]{kopper2019shadowit}, and the continuous monitoring of user needs to ensure suitable solutions are provided is a primary management recommendation \citep[26]{mallmann2018influence}. IS design principles should be established to reduce the workarounds that official systems generate \citep[3]{roeder2014managers}, and organizations should create a culture in which employees can openly surface their workarounds with managers, with appropriate communication channels and forums where benefits and risks can be assessed without prejudice \citep[120]{white2023workarounds}. Managers who understand the consequences of neutralizing \ac{SIT} can design more effective and efficient governance strategies \citep[1172]{trialih2023challenge}, while a comprehensive practitioner framework recommends accepting \ac{SIT}, assessing its threat to business continuity, enforcing cybersecurity policies with care, ensuring employees do not feel blocked, creating user experience teams, and even rewarding beneficial \ac{SIT} usage \citep[409]{haag2024dealing}. Managers should also assess why \ac{SIT} is used in their segments and what existing controls maintain the credibility of information it produces \citep[106]{myers2017impact}. Since \ac{IT} tools evolve continuously, organizations are compelled to re-evaluate their infrastructure regularly to ensure ongoing alignment with business needs \citep[373]{koeffer2015innovation}. Organizations should also increase agility and work to shift individual perceptions of the costs of \ac{SIT} systems, with pilot studies providing supervised \ac{SIT} access offering a means to build evidence-based governance understanding \citep[116]{silic2019critical}. Clear policies governing cloud \ac{IT} interaction are needed as organizational structural factors and cognitive strain are independent drivers of adoption \citep[74]{ogedengbe2024influence}. Managers deliberating governance must weigh the risk that abandoning \ac{SIT} constrains employee creativity and efficiency, with the persistence of negative attitudes toward \ac{SIT} even in the face of positive outcomes suggesting that institutional resistance may be more deeply entrenched than anticipated \citep[31, 38]{allen2019shadow}.

\subsubsection{Shadow AI}

As with \ac{SIT}, the governance of \ac{SAI} demands a multi-layered response that spans technical controls, governance frameworks, regulatory compliance, and cultural change. On the technical side, automated \ac{AI} discovery, inventory management, and advanced governance policy are identified as strategic countermeasures that establish organizational visibility over shadow systems \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}. The most advanced technical approach is Zero Trust Architecture, which treats all \ac{AI} assets as potential threats and requires constant validation and access control for each asset \citep[1]{puthal2025shadowai}. Effective risk management must also integrate technological safeguards such as \ac{AI}-aware \ac{DLP}, content filtering, data protection mechanisms, and \ac{AI} traffic monitoring \citep[2]{puthal2025shadowai}, while automated monitoring, anomaly detection, and policy enforcement mechanisms have been evaluated as effective tools for governing \ac{SAI} at scale \citep[1]{whitford2025aidriven}. Transparency within \ac{AI} systems is additionally critical: \ac{XAI} enhances transparency by making \ac{AI} decision-making interpretable, enabling organizations to identify and rectify biases or errors produced by shadow systems \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}.

At the governance framework level, organizations must establish comprehensive \ac{AI} governance structures incorporating compliance audits, \ac{AI} ethics boards, and regulatory enforcement mechanisms \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}. A typology of \ac{SAI} use cases combined with a risk classification framework has been proposed to enable organizations to categorize and prioritize governance responses \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}. The \ac{BYOAI}-GOVT framework offers a behavior-aware governance model that integrates task-risk zoning, distinguishing between Enable, Regulate, and Restrict categories, with a four-level organizational maturity scale, providing a structured path from informal adoption to responsible innovation \citep[2693]{anthuvan2025byoai}. Regular \ac{AI} compliance audits and risk assessments, as recommended by the \ac{NIST}, help organizations systematically identify vulnerabilities in their \ac{AI} landscape \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}, and regulatory agencies are urged to strengthen oversight by imposing stricter penalties for unauthorized \ac{AI} usage \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}. At the supranational level, the European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act categorizes \ac{AI} applications by risk level and imposes compliance obligations that strengthen organizational governance \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}.

Human-centered countermeasures are equally emphasized in the literature. Employee training programs are essential for raising awareness of unauthorized \ac{AI} usage and reinforcing adherence to cybersecurity policies, and \ac{AI} literacy programs tailored to specific sectors, particularly education, are needed to refine assessment methods and ensure academic integrity \citep[6]{balogun2025ethical}. Digital organizational culture plays a positive moderating role in the relationship between human \ac{AI} skills and organizational innovation \citep[1]{an2024impact}, suggesting that organizations which cultivate a supportive digital culture can channel \ac{AI} adoption toward sanctioned and innovative outcomes rather than covert workarounds. Nonetheless, challenges such as resistance to change and ethical concerns remain significant barriers to cultural alignment, even where \ac{AI} adoption promises clear organizational benefits \citep[1]{murire2024artificial}. Collectively, the literature advocates for a governance posture that neither prohibits \ac{AI} use outright nor ignores its risks, but instead creates structured pathways, through frameworks, training, technical safeguards, and cultural enablement, through which employees can adopt \ac{AI} responsibly and organizations can maintain meaningful oversight.

\section{Conceptual Development: Extending Shadow IT to Shadow AI} \label{conceptual-development}

Building on the previously established theoretical foundations and the insights of the \ac{SLR} results, this section focuses on the conceptual linkage and analytical extension of \ac{SIT} to \ac{SAI}. While \ac{SIT} has been extensively studied over the past decades, resulting in a well-established understanding of its underlying motivations, organizational consequences, and governance approaches, the emergence of \ac{SAI} presents a relatively novel phenomenon. Existing literature on \ac{SAI} is still limited in scope and fragmentation, offering only initial insights without a comprehensive conceptual framework.

To address this gap, this section adopts a transfer-oriented perspective. It systematically draws on the established body of knowledge on \ac{SIT} and examines how its core concepts, theories, and findings can be applied to the context of \ac{SAI}. This approach is based on the assumption that \ac{SAI}, while technologically more advanced, represents an evolution rather than a completely independent phenomenon. As such, similar organizational drivers and behavioral patterns are expected to persist, even though they may manifest differently due to the specific characteristics of \ac{AI} technologies.

At the same time, the transfer is not assumed to be one-to-one. \ac{AI} systems introduce new properties, such as increased autonomy, opacity, and scalability, that may alter both the nature and the impact of associated risks and opportunities. Therefore, the analysis not only seeks to identify parallels between \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} but also to highlight important deviations that emerge in the \ac{AI} context.

By structuring the conceptual development along the dimensions of motivations, consequences, and governance approaches, this section provides a theoretically grounded foundation for the subsequent analysis. It enables a systematic exploration of \ac{SAI} despite the current limitations of dedicated literature, while ensuring that the insights remain anchored in established research.

\subsection{Transferable Motivations} \label{transferable-motivations}

The motivational architecture that drives \ac{SIT} adoption does not dissolve when employees transition to \ac{AI}-based tools, it transfers, structurally intact, into the \ac{SAI} domain. The most fundamental driver in both phenomena is the existence of an unmet need arising from a gap between what the official \ac{IT} organization provides and what employees require to perform their work (\citealp[148]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[6]{ross2025shadowai}). In the \ac{SIT} context, this gap is rooted in the failure to provide adequate applications and the inability to fulfill business needs through legitimate channels \citep[6]{gyoery2012exploring}; in the \ac{SAI} context, the same structural logic produces identical behavior, as employees adopt unsanctioned \ac{AI} tools in direct response to perceived inefficiencies in officially sanctioned digital infrastructures \citep[2]{ross2025shadowai}.

The productivity and performance orientation is equally transferable. \ac{SIT} users are motivated by the promise of faster task completion and enhanced individual performance \citep[239]{pinto2023encourage}, while \ac{SAI} adopters are similarly driven by pressing demands for productivity gains, task automation, and augmented decision-making \citep[2, 3]{ross2025shadowai}. Both populations perceive their unofficial tools as more useful than sanctioned alternatives (\citealp[3]{mallmann2016behavioral}; \citealp[2]{chin2025conflicting}). Bureaucratic barriers and \ac{IT} department sluggishness are likewise a direct motivational antecedent in both contexts: \ac{SIT} emerges from formal development procedures, approval bottlenecks, and prohibitively high transaction costs of dealing with central \ac{IT} (\citealp[7]{zimmermann2014managing}; \citealp[9]{chua2014central}), while \ac{SAI} users feel that the agility gained through \ac{AI} experimentation justifies bypassing slower official approval processes \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}.

Policy unawareness and non-malicious intent also transfer directly. \ac{SIT} users frequently persist in non-compliant behavior because they do not understand applicable policies and do not perceive their behavior as harmful \citep[277-279]{silic2014shadow}; \ac{SAI} adopters equally conceal their usage when formal guidance is lacking and do not act with harmful intent \citep[2]{varshini2025facing}. The consumerization dynamic transfers as well: just as the proliferation of consumer-grade \ac{IT} tools lowered the adoption threshold for \ac{SIT} \citep[8]{thatte2012feral}, the widespread availability of intuitive, consumer-facing \ac{AI} services, accessible without expert technical knowledge \citep[436]{mallmann2021mediating}, has replicated that lowering of barriers specifically for \ac{AI} tools \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}.

\subsection{Novel Motivations} \label{novel-motivational-dimensions}

While the motivational foundations of \ac{SAI} are substantially inherited from \ac{SIT}, several drivers emerge that are genuinely novel and specific to the \ac{AI} context. The most distinctive is the skill gap compensation dynamic: employees resort to consumer-grade \ac{AI} solutions to actively compensate for technical weaknesses within their teams and use \ac{AI} capabilities to offset individual skill deficits that would not be addressable through conventional software tools (\citealp[1-2]{ross2025shadowai}; \citealp[3]{an2024impact}). This represents a qualitative departure from \ac{SIT} motivation, where employees sought tools that better matched their existing workflows, not tools that extended their cognitive or technical capabilities beyond their baseline competencies.

A second novel motivational dimension is the competitive and survival imperative associated with \ac{AI} adoption. In the era of the digital economy, rapid technological innovation and globalization make it effectively inevitable for organizations and individuals to adopt \ac{AI} in order to maintain long-term competitiveness \citep[1]{an2024impact}. This positions \ac{SAI} not merely as a workaround for operational inconvenience but as a perceived strategic necessity, a framing absent from the \ac{SIT} literature. \ac{SAI} tools also promise competitive advantage in a way that generic shadow software does not \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}, and their applicability spans a qualitatively broader range of knowledge work tasks, including decision-making, communication, information processing, coordination, and value creation \citep[11]{alter2024updated}, than the task-specific tools that characterize most \ac{SIT} adoption.

The governance drift zone constitutes a third novel motivational enabler. Unlike \ac{SIT}, which operates in contexts where formal \ac{IT} policies are broadly established, \ac{SAI} frequently emerges in organizational environments where \ac{AI} governance frameworks simply do not yet exist or exist only in name, creating a vacuum in which policy-violating behavior cannot technically occur (\citealp[1]{silic2025shadow}; \citealp[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}). The lack of policy awareness and vague approval mechanisms specific to \ac{AI} facilitate risk-taking behavior that would face clearer constraints in a mature \ac{IT} governance environment \citep[1140]{raizada2026shadowai}. Finally, the accessibility enabled by \ac{AI} model repositories such as Hugging Face Hub allows non-experts to rapidly prototype and deploy sophisticated models \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}, enabling a depth of unauthorized technical deployment that goes well beyond what was achievable in the consumer \ac{IT} consumerization wave that drove \ac{SIT}.

\subsection{Behavioral Shift in Knowledge Work} \label{behavioral-shift-in-knowledge-work}

The transition from \ac{SIT} to \ac{SAI} represents more than an incremental extension of unauthorized tool adoption, it reflects a qualitative shift in how employees relate to technology within knowledge work. \ac{SIT} was primarily adopted to enhance the efficiency of existing task structures: employees substituted better tools for inadequate ones, automating administrative tasks and improving access to information (\citealp[114]{white2023workarounds}; \citealp[2]{koch2014consumerization}). \ac{AI} tools, by contrast, are adopted to fundamentally augment cognitive work: they automate not merely repetitive manual tasks but analytical, communicative, and creative processes, drafting, summarizing, brainstorming, and decision support (\citealp[4]{ross2025shadowai}; \citealp[7]{silic2025shadow}). \ac{SAI} has been credited with sparking creativity and enabling employees to approach problem-solving in entirely new ways \citep[9]{silic2025shadow}, a consequence that extends well beyond the efficiency gains associated with \ac{SIT}.

Behaviorally, the adoption of \ac{SAI} is characterized by a culture of digital improvisation in which employees feel compelled to discover and deploy their own solutions, and this culture is fueled by desires for innovation, resistance to bureaucracy, and the informal social networks through which \ac{AI} tools are shared \citep[1, 3]{ross2025shadowai}. Psychological safety and innovation-seeking are identified as the primary behavioral drivers of experimentation with unmonitored \ac{AI} tools, a motivational profile that is more cognitively and culturally grounded than the operational frustration that typically underlies \ac{SIT} adoption \citep[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}. The perceived productivity gains of \ac{AI} tools also outweigh compliance concerns for the average employee, particularly under task pressure \citep[1136, 1140]{raizada2026shadowai}, creating a rationalization dynamic structurally similar to, but more psychologically entrenched than, the performance expectancy justifications documented for \ac{SIT} \citep[505]{nguyen2024understanding}. This behavioral pattern, in which innovation effectively bypasses organizational control and top-down prohibitions are rendered largely ineffective, signals that the governance challenge posed by \ac{SAI} is not merely an intensification of the \ac{SIT} problem but a structurally distinct behavioral phenomenon rooted in the nature of \ac{AI} itself \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}.

\subsection{Integrated Conceptual Framework} \label{integrated-conceptual-framework}

The findings from both systematic literature reviews enable the construction of an integrated conceptual framework that maps the evolution from \ac{SIT} to \ac{SAI} and identifies the structural continuities and discontinuities between the two phenomena. At the broadest level, both phenomena share a common causal architecture: they are jointly produced by a triad of enablers, motivators, and missing barriers \citep[22]{klotz2019causing}. The enabling conditions, consumer-grade tool accessibility, organizational resource constraints, and low technical barriers to adoption, are present in both contexts and represent the structural preconditions without which neither \ac{SIT} nor \ac{SAI} would emerge at scale. The motivators, unmet needs, performance pressure, bureaucratic friction, autonomy, and policy unawareness, operate across both phenomena with the additions of \ac{AI}-specific drivers (skill gap compensation, competitive necessity, governance vacuum) that elevate the intensity and breadth of \ac{SAI} adoption. The missing barriers, inadequate governance, weak social norms, absent or unenforced policies, are amplified in the \ac{SAI} context by the governance drift zone that characterizes the current organizational response to \ac{AI}.

The consequence structures are parallel in form but divergent in severity. Both phenomena produce a dual profile of risks and benefits: cybersecurity exposure, governance disruption, and operational inefficiency on the risk side; productivity gains, innovation, and enhanced communication on the benefit side (\citealp[13]{mallmann2018weare}; \citealp[1]{ross2025shadowai}). However, \ac{SAI} introduces consequence categories with no equivalent in the \ac{SIT} literature, including adversarial model attacks, algorithmic bias, sector- specific ethical liability, and knowledge leakage through generative outputs, that represent a qualitative escalation of organizational risk (\citealp[973]{puthal2025shadowai}; \citealp[3]{balogun2025ethical}; \citealp[1]{chin2025conflicting}).

At the governance level, the framework reveals an evolutionary trajectory: the  countermeasure portfolio established for \ac{SIT}, spanning deterrence, formalization, technical detection, training, and user-centric need fulfillment (\citealp[150-151]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[8-9]{gyoery2012exploring}), provides a foundational template that must be substantially extended for \ac{SAI}. New governance instruments, Zero Trust Architecture, \ac{AI}-aware \ac{DLP}, \ac{XAI}, the \ac{BYOAI}-GOVT framework, and supranational regulatory compliance with the EU \ac{AI} Act, are required not as replacements for the \ac{SIT} governance toolkit but as extensions of it, designed to address the novel risk dimensions that \ac{AI} introduces (\citealp[144]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[9623]{anthuvan2025byoai}; \citealp[7]{balogun2025ethical}). The integrated framework thus positions \ac{SAI} as a second-generation manifestation of the \ac{SIT} phenomenon, structurally continuous, but consequentially escalated and governance-inadequate by the standards of existing organizational practice.

\section{Comparative Analysis of Shadow IT and Shadow AI} \label{comparative-analysis}

\subsection{Differences in Motivational Drivers} \label{differences-in-motivational-drivers}

While \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} share a common motivational bedrock, a comparative analysis reveals meaningful differences in the depth, breadth, and psychological character of their respective driver profiles. In the \ac{SIT} context, motivations are predominantly operational in nature: employees adopt unauthorized tools primarily because official systems are inadequate, \ac{IT} departments are too slow, or the transaction costs of formal procurement are prohibitive (\citealp[7]{zimmermann2014managing}; \citealp[3]{keskin2021domesticating}). The motivational logic is essentially substitutive, employees replace inadequate official tools with better ones, and is anchored in concrete task-level frustrations such as system usability deficits, missing functionality, or inflexible legacy environments (\citealp[108]{myers2017impact}; \citealp[1]{nwankpa2024shadow}).

\ac{SAI} motivations, while sharing this substitutive logic, extend into cognitive and strategic dimensions that \ac{SIT} does not reach. Employees adopt \ac{AI} tools not only to substitute for missing capabilities but to extend their own cognitive capacity, compensating for skill gaps (\citealp[1-2]{ross2025shadowai}; \citealp[3]{an2024impact}), accessing creative and decision-support capabilities they could not otherwise achieve, and positioning themselves competitively within an \ac{AI}-driven economy \citep[105]{myers2017impact}. The psychological profile of \ac{SAI} adoption is also distinct: psychological safety and innovation-seeking are identified as primary behavioral drivers \citep[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}, whereas \ac{SIT} adoption is more typically driven by effort expectancy and performance expectancy within established task structures \citep[500, 505]{nguyen2024understanding}. Furthermore, \ac{SAI} benefits from a governance vacuum that \ac{SIT} generally does not: vague or entirely absent \ac{AI} approval mechanisms facilitate adoption in contexts where equivalent \ac{IT} policy gaps would already have been closed (\citealp[1140]{raizada2026shadowai}; \citealp[1]{silic2025shadow}). In sum, while both phenomena are rooted in the same structural misalignment between organizational \ac{IT} supply and employee demand, \ac{SAI} motivations are more culturally embedded, more cognitively oriented, and more strategically rationalized than those of \ac{SIT}.

\subsection{Differences in Organizational Risk} \label{differences-in-organizational-risk}

\ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} share a family of risks, cybersecurity exposure, data leakage, governance disruption, operational inefficiency, and regulatory liability, but differ substantially in the severity, tractability, and sectoral specificity of those risks. In the \ac{SIT} domain, cybersecurity risks are primarily structural: unauthorized tools expand the attack surface, fall outside \ac{IT} security oversight, and introduce malware or data sovereignty violations (\citealp[1]{nwankpa2024shadow}; \citealp[278-280]{silic2014shadow}). These risks, while serious, are broadly amenable to established \ac{IT} security controls, monitoring, policy enforcement, network traffic analysis, and access restriction (\citealp[1029]{silic2017perspective}; \citealp[644]{walterbusch2017missing}). The operational consequences of \ac{SIT}, technical debt, data inconsistencies, integration problems, and standardization failures, are similarly bounded by the scope of the unauthorized tools themselves, which typically address specific functional tasks within individual business units \citep[3]{kopper2018business}.

\ac{SAI} introduces a risk profile that is both broader and fundamentally harder to mitigate. At the technical level, \ac{SAI} systems generate qualitatively new attack vectors with no \ac{SIT} equivalent: shadow backdoor models can be engineered to facilitate adversarial attacks, and shadow data leak models can channel sensitive organizational data to unauthorized recipients in ways that conventional \ac{IT} security architecture is not designed to detect \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}. Model hallucination, the generation of plausible but factually incorrect outputs, introduces operational risks of a kind that do not arise from conventional software tools and that can corrupt organizational decision-making in ways that remain undetected for extended periods \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}. The risk of organizational knowledge leakage through generative \ac{AI} is also structurally distinct from the information security risks of \ac{SIT}: employees who input proprietary data into unsanctioned LLMs expose that data to third-party training pipelines and external servers in ways that conventional \ac{DLP} frameworks are not equipped to govern (\citealp[1]{chin2025conflicting}; \citealp[2]{puthal2025shadowai}).

The ethical and sector-specific risk dimensions represent perhaps the sharpest point of differentiation. \ac{SIT} governance challenges are primarily organizational and technical; \ac{SAI} governance challenges extend into questions of algorithmic fairness, individual rights, and societal harm. The unchecked deployment of \ac{AI}-driven credit scoring systems has produced discriminatory lending practices, while unauthorized \ac{AI} diagnostic tools in healthcare risk patient confidentiality and misdiagnosis \citep[2, 3]{balogun2025ethical}. The black-box nature of many \ac{AI} systems compounds these risks by making it structurally impossible to audit how decisions were reached (\citealp[1]{silic2025shadow}; \citealp[4]{balogun2025ethical}). No equivalent accountability gap exists in the \ac{SIT} risk literature, where the consequences of unauthorized tool use, while operationally significant, do not carry the same potential for discriminatory or legally liable outcome at scale.

\subsection{Differences in Governance Approaches} \label{differences-in-governance-approaches}

The governance approaches developed for \ac{SIT} provide a template that is partially transferable to \ac{SAI}, but the novel risk dimensions of \ac{AI} necessitate a substantially extended and redesigned governance architecture. \ac{SIT} governance literature converges on a spectrum from deterrence-focused approaches, formal and informal sanctions, policy enforcement, and active monitoring, to enabling approaches that legitimize user-driven \ac{IT} through formalization, co-governance, and need fulfillment (\citealp[8-9]{gyoery2012exploring}; \citealp[9]{zimmermann2016governing}). The central governance insight from the \ac{SIT} literature is that prohibition alone is ineffective: strict policies push \ac{SIT} further into secrecy rather than eliminating it (\citealp[472]{haag2017shadow}; \citealp[856]{klesel2019employees}), and the most effective responses combine technical controls with user-centric approaches that address the underlying needs driving adoption (\citealp[26]{mallmann2018influence}; \citealp[14]{kopper2019shadowit}).

This core insight transfers directly to \ac{SAI}, top-down prohibitions of \ac{AI} tools have similarly been found to be largely ineffective, driving usage further underground and creating a culture where innovation bypasses control \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}. However, the governance instruments required to operationalize this principle in an \ac{AI} context are substantially more sophisticated. Whereas \ac{SIT} governance relies on network traffic monitoring, software standardization, and access controls (\citealp[278]{silic2014shadow}; \citealp[9]{koch2014consumerization}), \ac{SAI} governance demands \ac{AI}-specific technical safeguards including Zero Trust Architecture, \ac{AI}-aware \ac{DLP}, content filtering, \ac{AI} traffic monitoring, and automated anomaly detection (\citealp[973, 974]{puthal2025shadowai}; \citealp[1]{whitford2025aidriven}). \ac{XAI} represents an entirely new governance instrument with no \ac{SIT} counterpart, enabling organizations to make \ac{AI} decision-making interpretable and to identify biases or errors produced by shadow systems \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}.

At the framework level, \ac{SIT} governance has developed structured typologies, including \ac{IT}-control, user-driven, and user-oriented approaches, as well as co-governance and instance-level responsibility allocation models (\citealp[8-9]{gyoery2012exploring}; \citealp[30]{klotz2019causing}), that provide a conceptual vocabulary for governing \ac{SAI}. The \ac{BYOAI}-GOVT framework extends this vocabulary into the \ac{AI} domain, integrating task-risk zoning across Enable, Regulate, and Restrict categories with a four-level organizational maturity scale \citep[2693]{anthuvan2025byoai}. \ac{SAI} governance also operates under a supranational regulatory layer that has no equivalent in the \ac{SIT} governance landscape: the EU Artificial Intelligence Act categorizes \ac{AI} applications by risk level and imposes binding compliance obligations, while the \ac{NIST} \ac{AI} Risk Management Framework mandates regular compliance audits and risk assessments \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}. These external regulatory instruments shift the governance calculus for \ac{SAI} beyond organizational risk management into the domain of legal liability and regulatory compliance, a dimension largely absent from the \ac{SIT} governance literature.

Finally, the human and cultural dimensions of governance differ in emphasis. \ac{SIT} governance emphasizes security policy communication, training, and the removal of structural barriers as mechanisms for closing the policy awareness gap (\citealp[12]{silvius2012factors}; \citealp[279]{silic2014shadow}). \ac{SAI} governance equally requires training and awareness programs, but extends these into domain-specific \ac{AI} literacy, including education-specific programs to ensure academic integrity \citep[6]{balogun2025ethical}, and the cultivation of a digital organizational culture that actively channels \ac{AI} skills toward sanctioned innovation \citep[1]{an2024impact}. Resistance to change and concerns about ethical alignment remain significant governance barriers specific to the \ac{AI} context, suggesting that \ac{SAI} governance must address not only the behavioral dynamics of non-compliance but the deeper cultural and ethical dimensions of \ac{AI} adoption that extend well beyond the scope of conventional \ac{IT} governance frameworks \citep[1]{murire2024artificial}.

\section{Discussion} \label{discussion}

The previous sections have established an extensive empirical and conceptual foundation through a systematic literature review documenting the motivations, consequences, and countermeasures of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}, followed by a conceptual analysis extending the \ac{SIT} framework to the emerging domain of \ac{SAI} and a comparative analysis of both phenomena. This discussion section synthesizes these findings into interpretive arguments organized around the three sub-questions of this thesis, before addressing the overarching main research question.

The first sub-question asked which motivational factors known from \ac{SIT} are transferable to \ac{SAI} and which are specific to the \ac{AI} context. The \ac{SLR} findings reveal that the motivational architecture underlying \ac{SIT} transfers to \ac{SAI} with a high degree of structural fidelity. The most fundamental driver in both phenomena is the gap between what the official \ac{IT} organization provides and what employees need to perform their work effectively. Unmet needs, bureaucratic slowness, and the high transaction costs of dealing with central \ac{IT} are the most prominent motivational clusters in the \ac{SIT} literature, and the \ac{SAI} literature documents the same structural logic almost verbatim: employees adopt unsanctioned \ac{AI} tools in direct response to perceived inefficiencies in officially sanctioned digital infrastructures and the inability to access adequate solutions through legitimate channels.

This finding carries an important interpretive implication: \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} are not primarily behavioral anomalies driven by individual risk tolerance or technological enthusiasm, but rational organizational adaptations to structural deficiencies in official \ac{IT} provision. Employees in both contexts do not generally perceive their behavior as harmful, but as necessary. This reframing has direct consequences for governance: organizations that respond to shadow behavior with penalties rather than structural solutions are addressing the symptom rather than the cause, which is consistent with the countermeasures \ac{SLR} finding that prohibition-based strategies are largely ineffective in both domains.

Beyond the transferable drivers, three motivational dimensions emerge as genuinely novel to \ac{SAI}. The skill gap compensation dynamic (employees adopting \ac{AI} tools to offset individual or team-level capability deficits) has no structural equivalent in the \ac{SIT} literature, where employees sought tools that better matched existing workflows rather than tools that augmented their cognitive capacity. The competitive survival imperative, the perception that \ac{AI} adoption is a strategic necessity for individual career positioning and organizational competitiveness, similarly elevates \ac{SAI} adoption beyond the operational frustration logic that predominantly characterizes \ac{SIT} motivation. Most significantly, the governance drift zone represents a motivational enabler specific to the \ac{AI} context: \ac{SAI} frequently emerges in environments where formal \ac{AI} governance frameworks do not yet exist or carry no operational traction, meaning that employees are not technically violating any enforceable policy. This condition, absent from the mature \ac{SIT} policy environment, makes \ac{SAI} adoption both more pervasive and more difficult to govern than \ac{SIT} was at a comparable stage of organizational diffusion.

The second sub-question asked in what ways \ac{SAI} consequences differ from or escalate beyond those documented for \ac{SIT}. The \ac{SLR} findings confirm that both phenomena produce a structurally parallel dual consequence profilem, cybersecurity exposure, governance disruption, and operational inefficiency on the risk side, alongside productivity gains, innovation benefits, and enhanced communication on the benefit side. This structural parallel reinforces the second-generation framing of \ac{SAI}: it inherits the consequence logic of \ac{SIT} rather than generating an entirely unrelated risk profile.

However, the severity, tractability, and ethical dimensions of \ac{SAI} consequences represent a qualitative escalation that the \ac{SIT} consequence literature does not anticipate. Three distinctions are particularly significant. First, \ac{SAI} introduces attack vectors with no \ac{SIT} equivalent: shadow backdoor models engineered for adversarial attacks and shadow data leak models capable of channeling sensitive organizational data to unauthorized recipients operate through mechanisms that conventional \ac{IT} security architecture was not designed to detect. Second, model hallucination, the generation of plausible but factually incorrect outputs, introduces a category of operational risk absent from the \ac{SIT} literature, one that can corrupt organizational decision-making in ways that remain undetected for extended periods and are attributable to no identifiable technical failure. Third, the knowledge leakage risk associated with generative \ac{AI} is structurally distinct from conventional data breach risks: employees who input proprietary information into unsanctioned LLMs may expose that data to third-party training pipelines in ways that existing \ac{DLP} frameworks are simply not equipped to govern.

The ethical and sector-specific dimensions of \ac{SAI} consequences represent the sharpest point of differentiation from \ac{SIT}. Where \ac{SIT} governance challenges are primarily organizational and technical in character, \ac{SAI} governance challenges extend into questions of algorithmic fairness, patient safety, academic integrity, and discriminatory lending, dimensions that carry legal liability and societal harm potential at a scale that no \ac{SIT} consequence ever approached. The black-box nature of many \ac{AI} systems compounds this by making it structurally difficult to audit how decisions were reached, closing the accountability loop that organizations typically rely on when assessing governance failures. These findings confirm that \ac{SAI} is not simply a more severe version of \ac{SIT} but a phenomenon that introduces consequence categories requiring new analytical frameworks and new governance instruments.

The third sub-question asked which countermeasures have been identified for \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} respectively, and to what extent \ac{SIT} governance approaches are transferable to \ac{SAI}. The countermeasures \ac{SLR} and comparative analysis reveal a pattern that mirrors the motivational findings: the foundational governance logic transfers, but the specific instruments required for \ac{SAI} extend substantially beyond what the \ac{SIT} toolkit provides.

The central governance insight from the \ac{SIT} literature, that prohibition alone is ineffective and may drive shadow behavior further into organizational secrecy, transfers directly to \ac{SAI}, where top-down restrictions have similarly been found to push usage underground rather than reducing it. The structural approaches that prove most effective for \ac{SIT}, proactive need fulfillment, formalization pathways that transition shadow systems into governed Business-Managed \ac{IT}, co-governance models that preserve business unit autonomy while restoring organizational visibility, and user-centric communication strategies, provide an equally valid foundation for \ac{SAI} governance. The \ac{IT}-control, user-driven, and user-oriented governance typologies established in the \ac{SIT} literature offer a conceptual vocabulary that the BYOAI-GOVT framework extends directly into the \ac{AI} domain, mapping \ac{AI} tool use across Enable, Regulate, and Restrict risk zones within a four-level organizational maturity scale. This continuity is practically significant: organizations that have invested in \ac{SIT} governance infrastructure are not starting from scratch in their response to \ac{SAI}, they possess institutional knowledge and governance architecture that provides a meaningful head start.

Where \ac{SAI} governance diverges from \ac{SIT} governance is in the specificity, sophistication, and regulatory embeddedness of the instruments required. Network traffic monitoring, software standardization, and access controls, the primary technical countermeasures for \ac{SIT}, are insufficient for \ac{SAI}, which requires \ac{AI}-specific safeguards including Zero Trust Architecture, \ac{AI}-aware \ac{DLP}, content filtering, automated anomaly detection, and Explainable \ac{AI} to make model decision-making interpretable and auditable. More fundamentally, \ac{SAI} governance operates under a supranational regulatory layer, including the EU Artificial Intelligence Act and the \ac{NIST} \ac{AI} Risk Management Framework, that has no equivalent in the \ac{SIT} governance landscape and shifts the calculus from organizational risk management into the domain of legal compliance and external accountability. This regulatory dimension means that the urgency of building \ac{SAI} governance infrastructure is not merely a matter of organizational risk tolerance but of legal obligation, a framing entirely absent from the \ac{SIT} countermeasures literature.

The main research question asked how \ac{SAI} relates to and differs from traditional \ac{SIT} with regard to motivational factors, consequences, and countermeasures. Synthesizing the findings across all three sub-questions, the answer is that \ac{SAI} is best understood as a second-generation manifestation of the \ac{SIT} phenomenon, structurally continuous in its causal logic but consequentially escalated and governance-inadequate by the standards of existing organizational practice.

The continuities are substantial. The fundamental triad of enablers, motivators, and missing barriers that produces \ac{SIT} operates identically in the \ac{SAI} context, and the core governance insight, that the energy driving shadow behavior must be channeled rather than suppressed, applies with equal force to both phenomena. Organizations that have learned from their \ac{SIT} experience are better equipped to manage \ac{SAI} than those that have not, and the \ac{SIT} governance toolkit remains a necessary, if insufficient, foundation for any effective \ac{SAI} response.

The discontinuities are equally real and must not be minimized. \ac{SAI} motivations extend into cognitive and strategic dimensions that \ac{SIT} motivations do not reach, making adoption more personally invested and more resistant to deterrence. \ac{SAI} consequences introduce entirely new risk categories, adversarial attacks, hallucination, algorithmic bias, generative knowledge leakage, that \ac{SIT} frameworks were not designed to capture. And \ac{SAI} governance must contend with a policy vacuum and a supranational regulatory layer that have no equivalent in the \ac{SIT} governance landscape.

These findings produce a clear practical implication: organizations should neither treat \ac{SAI} as a completely novel problem requiring entirely new frameworks, nor as a simple extension of \ac{SIT} that existing frameworks can address without modification. The appropriate response is a graduated, layered governance architecture that builds on the \ac{SIT} foundation, addressing root causes through structural need-fulfillment, deploying governance instruments specifically tweaked to \ac{AI}-specific risk profiles, and introducing an organizational culture that treats shadow behavior as an innovation signal rather than a compliance failure. As \ac{AI} becomes the primary medium through which knowledge work is conducted, the governance of unauthorized \ac{AI} adoption is not a peripheral \ac{IT} management concern but one of the defining organizational challenges of the coming decade, and one for which the accumulated knowledge of the \ac{SIT} research tradition provides both a starting point and a warning about the cost of responding too late.

\section{Limitations and Future Research Directions} \label{limitations}

While this review provides a comprehensive overview and valuable conceptual insights into motivations, consequences, and governance approaches of \ac{SAI} as a emerging topic, it is not without limitations.

First, the literature search was limited to four databases and specific search strings, which may have resulted in the exclusion of other relevant studies that use different terminology or appear in journals not covered by the databases. Although backward and forward searching was conducted to mitigate this risk, it cannot be completely ruled out. This limitation is particularly consequential for the \ac{SAI} literature, where terminology is still in progress and relevant contributions my be indexed under terms not captured by the search strings applied.

Second, the review was conducted by a single researcher, which may introduce potential bias in the selection, coding, and interpretation of the literature. The lack of consistent terminology around \ac{SAI} may have further affected the reliability of the systematic review process. While efforts were made to ensure academic rigor and objectivity throughout, the absence of multiple independent reviewers is a methodological constraint that limits the verifiability of the presented findings.

Third, the conceptual development and comparative analysis rest on the assumption that insights from \ac{SIT} are transferable to \ac{SAI}. While this assumption is empirically grounded in the structural similarities identified across both phenomena, it carries the risk of oversimplification in the context of \ac{AI} due to the different technological characteristics and types of \ac{AI} tools with their unique dimensions such as autonomous decision-makingm generative output, and cognitive augmentation. These capacities may produce organizational dynamics for which the \ac{SIT} framework provides insufficient explanatory power.

Fourth, the review is limited to literature published to the date of finishing the literature search (March 2026), which may not capture all relevant facets of the rapidly emerging field of \ac{SAI}. Also because this concept is still in its early stages, many of the findings are theoretically grounded propositions rather than empirical conclusions, and the field's understanding is likely to shift as more empirical research is conducted.

Fifth, the review does not include primary data collection. The conceptual framework developed in this thesis and the comparative interpretations drawn from the \ac{SLR} findings have not been tested in organizational contexts. The proposed framework and its claims accordingly require empirical validation before they can be applied in practice.

Given the findings and limitations outlined above, several directions for future research on this topic emerge from the identified gaps.

Empirical studies are needed to validate the transferability of \ac{SIT} to \ac{SAI} and to explore the \ac{AI}-specific characteristics and their impact on organizational processes and outcomes. While the structural parallels between \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} motivations and governance approaches are well-supported by the reviewed literature, it has not been tested whether employees who adopt unauthorized \ac{AI} tools are driven by the same motivational factors documented for \ac{SIT} users. Qualitative studies, such as case studies or interviews, could provide deeper insights into the changes in organizational practices and employee behavior, while quantitative studies could test the relevance and strength of identified relationships. 

A second important research direction concerns the application of behavioral theories already established in the \ac{SIT} literature, could also be applied to \ac{SAI} to better understand the psychological and social dynamic of unauthorized \ac{AI} adoption. Neutralization theory, which explains how employees construct cognitive justifications for non-compliance, and general strain theory, which frames unauthorized \ac{IT} adoption as coping response to structural stressors, have been applied to \ac{SIT} but not yet tested in the context of unauthorized \ac{AI} use. Given the finding, that \ac{SAI} adoption is more psychologically embedded than \ac{SIT} adoption, these framworks may prove particularly productive, but may require modifications to account for the stronger innovation-seeking and psychological safety dimensions which characterize \ac{SAI} behavior.

The long-term organizational progression of \ac{SAI} instances remain entirely unstudied. The literature on \ac{SIT} documents a variety of lifecycle dynamics, including the transition of shadow systems, once they are detected and taken care of, into \ac{BMIT}. No equivalent research exists for \ac{SAI}. Longitudinal case studies could explore how organizations identify, evaluate, and respond to \ac{SAI} instances over time, and would fill a critical gap by providing the empirical basis for refining the current governance frameworks.

Also the ethical and sector-specific consequences of \ac{SAI} (e.g. algorithmic bias in finance or diagnostic errors in healthcare) represent research domains in their own right. Cross-disciplinary research integration information systems, ethics, law, and sector-specific expertise is needed to address the accountability gaps that \ac{SAI} introduces when decision-making occurs outside organizational oversight. The black-box nature of many \ac{AI} systems makes this research critical in high-stakes environments, where the consequences extend beyond organizational risk into individual and societal harm.

Overall the field of \ac{SAI} is still in its early stages, and the breadth of open research questions reflects both the complexity of the phenomenon and the scale of the challenges for governance. This thesis contributes a first systematic mapping for the research agenda, but the empirical and theoretical work needed to build upon remains substantial.

\section{Conclusion} \label{conclusion}

This thesis set out to examine how \ac{SAI} relates to and differs from traditional \ac{SIT} in organizations, with a focus on motivational factors, organizational consequences, and countermeasures. This comparison, despite the practical urgency of the topic, has previously not been addressed in a systematic and academically manner. To address this gap, a \ac{SLR} was conducted to synthesize the existing literature on both phenomena across all three dimensions. The resulting foundation was then used to develop a conceptual framwork extending the \ac{SIT} framework to the \ac{SAI} domain, followed by a comparative analysis and a synthesis of the findings against the research questions. The key findings of this work are presented below, organized around their contributions and limitations.

From a research perspective, the thesis' central contribution is the development of a conceptual relationship between \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}. Two phenomena that share a common causal structure but diverge in ways that existing frameworks do not fully capture. The \ac{SIT} literature, accumulated over more than a decade, provides a rich and empirically grounded understanding on why employees adopt unauthorized tools, what consequences this behavior produces for organizations, and how governance can respond. This thesis demonstrates that this base of knowledge in its entirity is not obsolete nor directly transferable. Rather, it provides the necessary foundation \ac{SAI} research must build upon, and not a ready-made answer to the challenges \ac{SAI} presents. By mapping the boundaries of transferability, this thesis identifies which motivational, consequential, and governance findings can be carried over, which require modification, and which aspects of \ac{SAI} fall outside the scope of \ac{SIT}. Also it contributes a structured analytical bridge between the two phenomena.

The second research contribution is the conceptual positioning of \ac{SAI} itself. In the absence of an established definition, taxonomy or governance framework for \ac{SAI}, this thesis proposes that it is most acurately understood as a second-generation manifestation of the \ac{SIT} phenomenon. It shares the same structural continuity in its causal logic, but consequentially escalated and inadequate by the standards of existing organizational practice. This framing avoids two analytical errors that the literature risks falling into: treating \ac{SAI} as a completely novel phenomenon disconnected from prior knowledge, or treating it as a simple extension of \ac{SIT} that requires no new thinking approaches. Both of these framings lead to governance responses that are either too late or too weak. By conceptualizing \ac{SAI} as a second-generation \ac{SIT}, this thesis is able to generate a precise and actionable research agenda. It it able to identify which aspects of the \ac{SIT} framework are sufficient for understanding \ac{SAI}, where extensions are needed, and where \ac{SAI} needs completely new frameworks.

The findings of this thesis carry three practical implications for organizations, derived from the research conclusions rather than generic governance advices. First, shadow behaviour in both forms, \ac{IT} and \ac{AI}, should be understood as a signal for organizations rather than a compliance failure. The existing literature consistently depicts that employees who adopt unauthorized tools do so with no harmfull intent, but because the official \ac{IT} endowment fails to meet their operational needs. Organizations that respond to shadow behavior just by restricting and sanctioning are treating the symptom rather than the cause. Instead, organizations should improve \ac{IT} responsiveness, reduce friction in the procurement process, and create official channels for employees to request the tools needed to perform their work. This distinction is not merely theoretical as the literature on countermeasures consistently finds, that just by working against the symptoms, organizations risk making shadow behavior even less visible and push it further into the shadows.

Second, once confronted with \ac{SAI}, organizations should resist treating it as an entirely new problem that requires completely new solution approaches. The institutional knowledge accumulated through years of \ac{SIT} governance (formalization pathways, co-governance models, user-centric communication strategies, graduated policy frameworks) remains directly relevant and applicable as a governance foundation. The practical advantage this represents is not insignficant: organizations that have invested in \ac{SIT} governance infrastructure have a significant advantage in addressing the challenges \ac{SAI} entails over those that have not. Therefore the appropriate response is not to discard existing governance frameworks but to build on them by deploying \ac{AI}-specific instruments Zero Trust Architecture, AI-aware data protection mechanisms, and \ac{XAI} alongside the human and cultural interventions that the \ac{SIT} governance literature has already established as effective.

Third, the regulatory dimension of \ac{SAI} introduces an urgency that \ac{SIT} governance never carried in the same form. The EU Artificial Intelligence Act and emerging international \ac{AI} risk frameworks mean that the governance of unauthorized \ac{AI} use is not solely an organizational risk management decision but increasingly a matter of legal compliance. Organizations that delay building \ac{SAI} governance infrastructure are not merely accepting elevated risk; they are accumulating legal exposure. This regulatory pressure, combined with the governance drift zone that currently characterizes most organizational \ac{AI} environments, means that the window for proactive governance is narrowing. The organizations best positioned to manage it are those that act on existing knowledge now, rather than waiting for the \ac{SAI} research literature to reach the maturity that \ac{SIT} took a decade to achieve.

Viewed in broader perspective, this thesis reflects a pattern that is common and has recurred accross the history of \ac{IT} management. Technological capabilities consistently outpace the organizational and governance structures designed to manage it. \ac{SIT} also emerged from this gap in the early 2000s and took years for both research and practice to discover, understand and address effectively. \ac{SAI} represents the same pattern at a higher level of technological complexity, cognitive depth and societal consequence. The accumulated knowledge on the \ac{SIT} phenomenon provides a rare opportunity for organizations to avoid repeating the same mistakes by responding to the governance challenges building on existing insights. Wether organizations take this opportunity will be determinded once the \ac{SAI} research field matures. This thesis aims to provide a structured, analytical foundation on which the choice can be made with greater clarity and evidence than was available to those facing the challenges of \ac{SIT} for the first time.
