\section{Theoretical Foundations - 6-8 pages} \label{theoretical-foundations}

In order to establish a comprehensive understanding of \ac{SIT} and its further, \ac{SAI}, this section provides an overview of the theoretical foundations and definitions relevant to these phenomena.

\subsection{Shadow IT} \label{shadow-it}

\subsubsection{Concept and Definition}

\ac{SIT} is a multifaceted phenomenon encompassing the use, development, or deployment of \ac{IT} artifacts (e.g., software, hardware, or services) outside the formal control, approval, or awareness of the central \ac{IT} function. For instance, \citet[1]{haag2017shadow} describe it as "hardware, software, or services built, introduced, and/or used for the job without explicit approval or even knowledge of the organization", whereas \citet[1]{kopper2016towards} emphasize autonomous deployment within business departments without IT involvement. Other authors similarly highlight the lack of alignment with IT governance (\citealp[4]{kaess2021towards}; \citealp[4]{rakovic2020shadow}).

Beyond these operational perspectives, \ac{SIT} can be conceptualized as a structural complement to formal IT, consisting of independently developed and used systems, processes, and practices that are not known, supported, or accepted by the official IT department (\citealp[147]{rakovic2020shadow}). More concise definitions further highlight either the user-driven development outside central IT or the absence of approval and monitoring as defining features (\citealp[4]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[2]{magunduni2018revisiting}).

Collectively, these definitions establish \ac{SIT} as a theoretically grounded construct characterized by autonomy, decentralization, and non-sanctioned IT activity, emerging outside formal IT governance.

\subsubsection{Scope}

\ac{SIT} encompasses a wide range of tools and solutions, including spreadsheets, databases, cloud services, mobile devices, and other ad-hoc \ac{IT} solutions (\citealp[2]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[1]{kopper2016towards}). These systems are typically small-scale, developed by individuals or small teams, and tailored to immediate operational needs \citep[2]{rakovic2020shadow}. Their emergence reflects both the accessibility of modern \ac{IT} tools and the flexibility required by employees in dynamic work environments.

\subsubsection{Conceptual Distinctions}

While \ac{SIT} shares similarities with related practices such as workarounds, and \ac{BYOD} programs, it is distinct in being unauthorized and unsanctioned. Workarounds represent behavioral adaptations to organizational constraints, whereas \ac{SIT} manifests these adaptations through technological artifacts. IT consumerization, on the other hand, refers to the broader trend of user-driven technology adoption, providing a context in which \ac{SIT} can emerge (\citealp[2]{rakovic2020shadow}; \citealp[2]{haag2017shadow}).

\subsubsection{Core Characteristics}

\ac{SIT} is not necessarily malicious or risky in intention. Users often deploy it to enhance productivity, overcome system limitations, or facilitate collaboration. Its defining features are low visibility, lack of \ac{IT} support, and a tension between organizational control and individual initiative.

\section{Methodology - 2-3 pages} \label{methodology}

The review follows a structured approach based on the guidelines provided by \citet[887-902]{okoli2015guide} for conducting a \ac{SLR} in the field of information systems research. The methodology is organized into four phases of two steps each: Planning, Selection, Extraction, and Execution \citep[885]{okoli2015guide}.

While the original framework is designed for collaborative research settings involving multiple reviewers, this review was conducted by a single researcher. Therefore, minor adaptions were made to certain steps (e.g., drafting the research protocol, team training) to ensure methodological rigor while maintaining the integrity of the review process. The following sections describe these phases and their implementation in the context of this review.

\subsection{Purpose} \label{purpose}

The first step in conducting a \ac{SLR} is to define the purpose of the review, which is to identify and analyze existing literature on the phenomenon of \ac{SIT} and further the development of \ac{SAI} in organizational contexts. The \ac{SLR} aims to synthesize insights on the motivations behind \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}, their organizational consequences, and the governance approaches employed to manage these phenomena.

\subsection{Draft Protocol} \label{draft-protocol}

The second step includes drafting a research question, a research protocol, and training the team. Since this thesis is conducted by a single person, the protocol and team training steps are not required and will therefore not be carried out further. The research questions guiding this review are as follows:

\textbf{Main Research Question (RQ):}
How does \ac{SAI} differ from traditional \ac{SIT} in organizations with regard to motivational factors, consequences, and countermeasures?

To better address the research question, it will be broken down into three sub-questions and answered throughout the paper: 

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Which motivational factors known from \ac{SIT} are transferable to \ac{SAI}, and which additional motivation factors are specific to \ac{SAI}?
  \item Which organizational consequences of \ac{SAI} differ from those of \ac{SIT}?
  \item Which countermeasures are discussed for \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}, and how do they differ in design and expected effectiveness?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Practical Screening} \label{practical-screening}

To make sure only relevant and high-quality literature is included in the review, practical screening criteria are defined to filter the large number of initial search results before reviewing them in detail.

For this review, only journal articles, conference proceedings, and book chapters/sections published in English between 2005 and 2026 with a focus on \ac{SIT}/\ac{SAI} in organizational or professional settings were included. The research design of the included studies could be empirical (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods), theoretical, conceptual, or review-based, as long as they provide insights relevant to the research questions. The participants in the included studies could be professionals, managers, employees in various roles (e.g., analysts, data scientists, IT staff), or end-users as long as they have a close connection to the organizational context of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}.

\subsection{Literature Search} \label{literature-search}

The literature search was conducted using four search strings across four databases (AISeL, EBSCO, IEEE, and Web of Science), employing advanced search with Boolean operators (\texttt{OR} and \texttt{AND}) applied to all fields or title/abstract/keywords depending on the database. The search strings were designed to capture literature on both concepts, as well as their intersection, to ensure a broad and comprehensive coverage of relevant studies. The search strings and their purposes are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:search-strings}. \subsection{Data Extraction} \label{data-extraction}

The remaining literature was subjected to a systematic data extraction process, where the literature was coded and sorted based on their key scope in accordance to the research questions. Based on this preliminary coding, relevant information was extracted and collected in a structured format to group similar concepts and themes together. This process was conducted iteratively, with multiple rounds of coding and extraction to ensure that all relevant information was captured and organized effectively for further analysis and synthesis in the subsequent phases of the review.

\subsection{Quality Appraisal} \label{quality-appraisal}

While the practical screening criteria help filter out irrelevant studies before even reading the full-text, the review process also involves a quality appraisal to ensure the included studies meet certain standards. Therefore exclusion criteria need to be defined and applied to the extracted studies \citep[884]{okoli2015guide}. 

This \ac{SLR} excluded studies with unsufficient theoretical contribution, unclear methodology or findings, or low prerequisites to adequately answer the research questions.

\subsection{Study Synthesization} \label{study-synthesization}

Study synthesization involves aggregating, summarizing, and interpreting the findings from the included studies to answer the research questions \citep[899]{okoli2015guide}. The aggregation utilized the preliminary outline of the review, which was based on the research questions, to group similar concepts and themes, such as definitions, motivations, consequences, and countermeasures of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI}. \footnote{To support the aggregation and summarization of relevant data, generative AI tools such as ChatGPT (OpenAI, GPT-5.3, April 2026 version) and Claude (Antrophic, Sonnet 4.5, April 2026 version) were used. All outputs were critically reviewed and revised by the author who assumes full responsibility for the final content.}


\subsection{Writing the Review} \label{writing-the-review}

The final phase of the review process presents the results in detail, so future reviewers can understand and reproduce the process and the findings \citep[901]{okoli2015guide}. Section~\ref{slr-results} presents the results of the \ac{SLR}, organized around the key themes of identified literature, motivational factors, organizational consequences, and governance approaches of \ac{SIT} and \ac{SAI} to summarize existing literature and knowledge about these concepts.

\subsection{Motivational Factors} \label{motivational-factors}

The following presents the different orientations of motivational factors.

\subsubsection{Shadow IT}

\subsubsection{Shadow AI}

The literature on \ac{SAI} suggests that the unauthorized usage of \ac{AI} tools is rarely driven by a desire to cause harm. Instead, it is characterized as a functional, user-driven response to specific workplace demands where official \ac{IT} protocols are avoided for personal and professional profit \citep[3]{silic2025shadow}. This section explores the multi-faceted motivations behind this behavior, ranging from individual productivity needs to systematical organizational failures.

The most dominant driver identified across the literature is the immediate pursiut of efficiency. Employees frequently describe \ac{AI}-powered tools as "game changers" for automating repetitive tasks and streamlining workflows \citep[9]{silic2025shadow}. This behavior is often percieved as beneficial by the users because it improves overall efficieny and provides analytical insigts that sanctioned tools may lack \citep[7]{silic2025shadow}.

In practical application, generative AI is adopted to achieve significant time-saving enhancements in several core areas:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Communication and Synthesis:} Employees use tools to draft emails, summarize lengthy documents, and brainstorm new ideas \citep[4]{ross2025shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Decision Augmentation:} \ac{AI} is leveraged to assist in decision-making, information processing, and coordination complex tasks \citep[11]{alter2024updated}.
  \item \textbf{Routine Automation:} Workers turn to these tools because they significantly enhance their productivity by automating routine tasks and accelerate human decision-making \citep[3]{ross2025shadowai}.
\end{itemize}

Research indicates that the "pressing demand for productivity gains" is a primary motivator for employees \citep[2]{ross2025shadowai}. This is especially true under high task presure, where usage becomes almost entirely efficiency-driven \citep[1140]{raizada2026shadowai}. Furthermore, the psychological safety and percieved productivity benefits often outweigh compliance concerns for the average worker \citep[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}. Thus, employees seek out third-party \ac{AI} tools even in sensitive industries like healthcare to fill gaps left by official resources (\citealp[2]{balogun2025ethical}; \citealp[2]{varshini2025facing}).

Another significant factor for the adoption of \ac{SAI} is the frustration stemming from traditional \ac{IT} management. Many employees turn to unsactioned tools as a direct response to percieved inefficiencies in their organization's sanctioned digital infrastructure \citep[2]{ross2025shadowai}.

Specific organizational drivers include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bureaucratic Friction:} Adoption is often fueled by bureaucratic bottlenecks, unresponsiveness from centralized systems, and a misalignment between official \ac{IT} infrastructure and actual operational needs \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Resource Scarcity:} The informal adoption of \ac{AI} is frequently an operational necessity born from limited technical resources \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Governance Failure:} A "governance drift zone" often exists where formal policies are present but lack real-world traction, leading employees to find their own solutions \citep[1]{silic2025shadow}.
\end{itemize}

When faced with bureaucratic inertia or a lack of accessible, sanctioned alternatives, workers feel compelled to bypass traditional \ac{IT} procurement to adopt sophisticated \ac{LLM}s for their daily tasks (\citealp[6]{ross2025shadowai}; \citealp[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}). This creates a situation where employees who use unauthorized \ac{AI} report different perceptions of task burden and work pressure compared to those who strictly follow protocols \citep[5]{varshini2025facing}.

Beyond simple efficiency, \ac{SAI} is driven by a desire for professional agility and the freedom to innovate. Users claim that leveraging these technologies enables them to be more innovative at work \citep[2]{chin2025conflicting}. For many, \ac{AI} has "sparked creativity", allowing them to approach problem-solving from new perspectives \citep[9]{silic2025shadow}.

The "innovation-seeking" behavior creates a workplace culture where the agility gained through experimentaion with \ac{AI} is seen as a justification for bypassing slow, official approval processes \citep[1136, 1138]{raizada2026shadowai}. In industries like healthcare, finance, and education, the drive for innovation and agility is a core reason for seeking alternative tools \citep[16]{balogun2025ethical}. This culture of "digital improvisation" allows innovation to effectively "bypass control" often driving usage further underground once the tools are banned (\citealp[1]{ross2025shadowai}; \citealp[1136]{raizada2026shadowai}).

\ac{SAI} also serves as a strategic tool for individual and team survival. At a technical level, employees use \ac{AI} skills to compensate for specific technical weaknesses \citep[3]{an2024impact}. Teams often resort to consumer-graade \ac{AI} solutions to actively bridge existing skill gaps \citep[1-2]{ross2025shadowai}.

From a competitive standpoint, the adoption of these tools is viewed as a way to create new opportunities for competitive advantage \citep[973]{puthal2025shadowai}. In the modern digital economy, characterized by rapid technological innovation and globalizationm mnany employees feel it is inevitable and necessary for the firm's long-term survival, to adopt \ac{AI} technology \citep[1]{an2024impact}. Key user motivations are often linked to a desire for responsible innovation and skills development that the organization may not yet formally support \citep[2708-2709]{anthuvan2025byoai}.

Finally, the absence of clear policy often acts as a silent enabler. Many employees hide their \ac{AI} usage specifically because formal guidance or policy is lacking \citep[2]{varshini2025facing}. This combination of low policy awareness and vague approval mechanisms facilitates risk-taking behavior among employees \citep[1138, 1140]{raizada2026shadowai}. In many cases, shadow systems emerge as a direct result of the policy absence, \ac{IT} bottlenecks, and general employee frustration \citep[1138]{raizada2026shadowai}.

\subsection{Organizational Consequences} \label{organizational-consequences}

\subsubsection{Shadow IT}



\subsubsection{Shadow AI}

While the adoption of \ac{SAI} is often driven by the pursuit of individual efficiency, its unsanctioned nature introduces a wide array of organizational, technical, and ethical repercussions. The literature indicates that the consequences of these practices extend far beyond simple policy violations, impacting the core security architecture, legal standing, and cultural fabric of contemporary organizations.

The most immediate technical consequence of \ac{SAI} is the introduction of systemic vulnerabilities that fundamentally challenge traditional cybersecurity models \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}. By bypassing formal vetting processes, these tools significantly expand the attack surface available to cybercriminals, making their presence a critical concern for security professionals \citep[5]{balogun2025ethical}.

Specific technical risks identified in the literature include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Adversarial Exploitation:} There is a growing risk of "shadow backdoor models" being engineered specifically to facilitate adversarial attacks against the organization \citep[1]{puthal2025shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Operational Disruptions:} The proliferation of unsanctioned systems poses risks that range from data breaches to significant operational disruptions \citep[1]{whitford2025aidriven}.
  \item \textbf{Infrastructure Fragility:} Because these tools are not integrated into official monitoring systems, they create "blind spots" that undermine the overall resilience of the corporate IT environment \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}.
\end{itemize}

\ac{SAI} usage frequently results in the erosion of data privacy and the violation of complex regulatory frameworks. A primary concern is "data leakage", where sensitive business information is inadvertently shared with unauthorized recipients through third-party AI models (\citealp[1]{puthal2025shadowai}; \citealp[1]{raizada2026shadowai}).

The consequences for data sovereignty are particularly severe:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Legal Violations:} Unauthorized AI usage often breaks data residency and sovereignty laws, as sensitive information is frequently moved to and stored in jurisdictions that do not meet required security standards \citep[2]{puthal2025shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Regulatory Non-Compliance:} Unmonitored use of Large Language Models (LLMs) leads to documented "compliance breaches" that can trigger significant legal penalties \citep[1]{raizada2026shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Knowledge Leakage:} Beyond individual data points, there is a broader risk of Organizational Knowledge Leakage (OKL), where proprietary intellectual property and strategic insights are exposed through employee interactions with external AI \citep[1]{chin2025conflicting}.
\end{itemize}

The social and cultural repercussions of \ac{SAI} are equally significant, often manifesting as a breakdown in institutional trust. The literature highlights that unauthorized use has profound repercussions on team dynamics and the overall trustworthiness of the workplace environment \citep[1]{varshini2025facing}. 

The phenomenon of "Bring Your Own AI" (BYOAI) is noted for creating persistent tensions between the desire for innovation, the necessity of trust, and the requirements of regulatory oversight \citep[1]{anthuvan2025byoai}. This often leads to a "governance drift zone", a state where formal policies exist on paper but lack any real-world traction or influence over employee behavior \citep[1]{silic2025shadow}. This drift creates a disconnect between management and staff, potentially fostering a culture of secrecy rather than collaborative innovation.

A critical consequence in high-risk fields is the emergence of responsibility and accountability gaps. Because many \ac{SAI} systems function as "black boxes", it becomes nearly impossible for organizations to determine exactly how certain decisions were reached (\citealp[4]{balogun2025ethical}; \citealp[1]{silic2025shadow}).

This lack of transparency exposes institutions to significant legal liability, particularly when AI-driven decisions lead to negative outcomes. In these scenarios, the inability to provide a clear audit trail or explanation for an AI's output makes it difficult to assign responsibility, leaving the organization legally vulnerable \citep[4]{balogun2025ethical}.

The consequences of \ac{SAI} are particularly acute in sensitive sectors where the margin for error is minimal:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Healthcare:} The use of unregulated AI diagnostic tools can lead to misdiagnoses, malpractice, and catastrophic breaches of patient confidentiality \citep[2]{balogun2025ethical}.
  \item \textbf{Finance:} In the financial sector, unregulated AI-driven credit scoring systems have already resulted in discriminatory lending practices, subsequently prompting intense regulatory scrutiny \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}.
  \item \textbf{Education:} Within academic environments, unauthorized AI applications contribute to academic dishonesty, flawed grading mechanisms, and the violation of student privacy rights \citep[2]{balogun2025ethical}.
\end{itemize}

Finally, there is a broader societal consequence: the unchecked expansion of \ac{SAI} risks entrenching systemic biases. If left unmanaged, these tools may further marginalize vulnerable populations by automating and scaling discriminatory logic that has not been subjected to ethical review \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}. In addition to bias, organizations must contend with "model hallucination", where AI produces confident but entirely false information, leading to flawed outputs and compromised decision-making \citep[1]{raizada2026shadowai}.

\subsection{Governance Approaches and Countermeasures} \label{governance-approaches-and-countermeasures}

\subsubsection{Shadow IT}

\subsubsection{Shadow AI}

As organizations recognize that total prohibition of \ac{SAI} is often impractical and counterproductive, the literature has shifted toward a more nuanced spectrum of countermeasures. These range from high-level strategic frameworks to granular technical monitoring, all aimed at transitioning from a state of "shadow" usage to a sanctioned, transparent environment.

The first line of defense against the risks of \ac{SAI} involves technical visibility. Organizations are increasingly evaluating the effectiveness of automated monitoring and anomaly detection to identify unsanctioned \ac{AI} traffic \citep[1]{whitford2025aidriven}. Effective risk management requires the integration of "AI-aware" safeguards, specifically Data Loss Prevention (DLP) tools, content filtering, and dedicated \ac{AI} traffic monitoring \citep[2]{puthal2025shadowai}.

A fundamental technical countermeasure is the implementation of automated \ac{AI} discovery and inventory systems \citep[1]{puthal2025shadowai}. These systems allow IT departments to maintain a clear record of which tools are being accessed, which is a prerequisite for applying any advanced governance policy. By providing a real-time overview of the organization's \ac{AI} footprint, these tools help eliminate the "blind spots" traditionally associated with shadow systems.

Beyond simple monitoring, the literature proposes structural changes to how \ac{AI} assets are handled within the corporate network. One of the most advanced technical approaches is the adoption of a Zero Trust Architecture \citep[1]{puthal2025shadowai}. Under this model, every \ac{AI} asset—regardless of whether it is internal or a third-party application—is treated as a potential threat. This requires constant validation and rigorous access control for every single interaction \citep[1]{puthal2025shadowai}.

Furthermore, the "black box" nature of many \ac{SAI} tools is addressed through the promotion of \ac{XAI}. \ac{XAI} serves as a critical countermeasure by making \ac{AI} decision-making processes interpretable for human supervisors \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}. This transparency allows organizations to identify and rectify biases or errors in \ac{AI} outputs before they result in operational or legal harm \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}.

The literature emphasizes that technical tools must be embedded within broader strategic governance frameworks. For instance, the \textbf{BYOAI-GOVT} framework offers a behavior-aware model that categorizes tasks into "risk zones": \textit{Enable}, \textit{Regulate}, and \textit{Restrict} \citep[1]{anthuvan2025byoai}. This framework allows for a tiered response to \ac{AI} usage based on a four-level maturity scale, moving away from binary "yes/no" policies \citep[1]{anthuvan2025byoai}.

Other researchers suggest that a successful governance structure must include:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Risk Classification:} Developing a typology of \ac{SAI} use cases to understand and rank specific threats \citep[1]{ross2025shadowai}.
  \item \textbf{Organizational Bodies:} Establishing \ac{AI} ethics boards and dedicated compliance units to oversee implementation \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}.
  \item \textbf{Continuous Auditing:} Implementing regular \ac{AI} compliance audits and risk assessments to identify emerging vulnerabilities \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}.
\end{itemize}

Because \ac{SAI} is fundamentally a human behavior, several countermeasures focus on education and culture. Employee training programs are deemed essential for raising awareness about the dangers of unauthorized \ac{AI} and reinforcing cybersecurity policies \citep[6]{balogun2025ethical}. In academic and research settings, this extends to "\ac{AI} literacy programs" designed to ensure that the use of generative tools does not compromise academic integrity \citep[6]{balogun2025ethical}.

However, education alone is insufficient if it is not supported by the organizational culture. A digital organizational culture plays a vital "positive moderating role," helping to align individual \ac{AI} skills with authorized organizational innovation \citep[1]{an2024impact}. Management must address cultural challenges, such as employee resistance to change and deep-seated ethical concerns, to ensure that new governance policies are actually followed rather than circumvented \citep[1]{murire2024artificial}.

Finally, internal governance is increasingly being dictated by external regulatory requirements. The literature points to the \textbf{European Union’s Artificial Intelligence Act} as a primary driver for compliance, as it categorizes \ac{AI} applications by risk level and imposes strict legal obligations on organizations \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}. Adhering to established standards, such as those provided by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), is recommended as a best practice for conducting thorough risk assessments \citep[7]{balogun2025ethical}.

Furthermore, there is a call for regulatory agencies to take a more active role by strengthening oversight and imposing stricter penalties for the unauthorized use of \ac{AI}, particularly in high-stakes industries \citep[3]{balogun2025ethical}. By combining legal pressure with internal auditing, organizations can create a more robust environment where the benefits of AI are realized without the risks of shadow implementation.

\section{Limitations} \label{limitations}

While this review provides a comprehensive overview and valuable conceptual insights into motivations, consequences, and governance approaches of \ac{SAI} as a emerging topic, it is not without limitations.

First, the literature search was limited to four databases and specific search strings, which may have resulted in the exclusion of other relevant studies that use different terminology or appear in journals not covered by the databases. Although backward and forward searching was conducted to mitigate this risk, it cannot be completely ruled out.

Second, the review was conducted by a single researcher, which may introduce potential bias in the selection, coding, and interpretation of the literature. While efforts were made to ensure rigor and objectivity, the absence of multiple reviewers may limit the reliability of the presented fidings.

Third, the conceptual development and comparative analysis are based on the assumption of a transferability of insights from \ac{SIT} with the risk of oversimplification in the context of \ac{AI} due to the different technological characteristics and types of \ac{AI} tools.

Fourth and finally, the review is limited to literature published to the current date (March 2026), which may not capture all relevant facets of the emerging field of \ac{SAI}.

\section{Future Research Directions} \label{future-research-directions}

Given the findings and limitations outlined above, there is ample scope for future research on this topic. Empirical studies are needed to validate the transferability of \ac{SIT} to \ac{SAI} and to explore the \ac{AI}-specific characteristics and their impact on organizational processes and outcomes. Qualitative studies, such as case studies or interviews, could provide deeper insights into the changes in organizational practices and employee behavior, while quantitative studies could test the relevance and strength of identified relationships.

Although many forms of \ac{SAI} can be derived from and build upon the foundations of \ac{SIT}, \ac{AI} poses some unique risks that have either already been studied, have not yet been identified, or have yet to emerge. For this reason, more in-depth and specific long-term research is needed.

Overall the field of \ac{SAI} is still in its early stages, calling for a wide range of research areas (e.g. socio-technical, cybersecurity, ethics) to be explored.
